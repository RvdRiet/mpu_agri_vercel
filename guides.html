Crop Farming Guides

Crop Production in South Africa

Comprehensive guides for all major crops grown in South Africa, covering ideal growing conditions, soil requirements, planting, fertilisation, pest management, and harvest.

Sources: DAFF, ARC-Grain Crops, ARC-ITSC, Grain SA, SASRI, CRI, SUBTROP, Potatoes SA, SANSOR.

Maize Production Guide

Maize (Zea mays) is South Africa's most important field crop, with approximately 2.5 million hectares planted annually. Mpumalanga's Highveld — Ermelo, Bethal, Standerton, Middelburg, and Carolina — is a core production region. The summer rainfall pattern (October–March) and deep, fertile soils make it ideal for dryland maize, while the Lowveld supports irrigated production.

White vs yellow maize: White maize is the primary human food staple in SA (mealie meal); yellow maize is used predominantly for animal feed and industrial processing. White maize generally fetches a premium on SAFEX.

Open-pollinated (OPV) vs hybrid: OPVs (e.g., Afric 1, Nelson's Choice) are cheaper and farmers can save seed, but yields are 20–40% lower than hybrids. Hybrids (e.g., from Pannar/Corteva, DEKALB/Bayer, Pioneer, Sensako) deliver consistent high yields but require fresh seed each season.

Bt maize: Contains the Bacillus thuringiensis gene for stalk borer resistance. Very effective against African stalk borer (Busseola fusca) but offers only partial control of fall armyworm (Spodoptera frugiperda). Always plant a 5% non-Bt refuge strip to delay resistance evolution.

Drought-tolerant hybrids: ARC and CIMMYT-derived Water Efficient Maize for Africa (WEMA) cultivars are available for marginal rainfall areas. These cultivars maintain yields 20–30% better under moderate drought stress than conventional hybrids.

Maturity groups: Ultra-short (under 110 days) for late planting or short seasons; medium (120–140 days) for the Mpumalanga Highveld; long (145+ days) only under irrigation or in high-rainfall areas. Match cultivar maturity to your region's growing season length — consult the ARC-Grain Crops cultivar evaluation booklet released annually.

Cultivar recommendations for Mpumalanga Highveld: Medium-maturity hybrids with grey leaf spot (GLS) resistance are essential. Prioritise cultivars with documented stability data from ARC national cultivar trials at Bethal and Ermelo test sites.

Temperature: Optimum range 18–27°C during the growing season. Soil temperature at planting depth must exceed 10°C for germination; 12°C is preferred. Night temperatures consistently below 10°C slow growth significantly. Daytime temperatures above 35°C during flowering reduce pollination and kernel set.

Rainfall: Dryland maize requires 500–800mm of well-distributed rainfall during the growing season. Peak water demand (6–8mm/day) occurs during the tasselling and silking stages (approximately 8–10 weeks after emergence). A mid-season drought during this period can reduce yields by 40–60%.

Frost-free period: At least 120–140 days of frost-free conditions for medium-maturity cultivars. The Mpumalanga Highveld typically receives first frost in late April/May and last frost in September/early October.

Altitude: Most Mpumalanga maize is grown at 1,400–1,800m above sea level on the Highveld. Higher altitudes have cooler temperatures and shorter growing seasons — use shorter-maturity cultivars above 1,700m.

Mpumalanga regions suited: Ermelo, Bethal, Standerton, Delmas, and Middelburg form the core of the province's maize belt. Carolina and Hendrina are marginal but viable with adapted cultivars. The Lowveld (below 600m) is too hot for dryland maize but supports irrigated production.

Ideal pH: 5.5–7.0 (measured in KCl). Below pH 5.0, aluminium toxicity inhibits root growth and phosphorus becomes unavailable. Most Mpumalanga Highveld soils are naturally acidic (pH 4.5–5.5) and require liming.

Texture: Well-drained loam to clay-loam is ideal. Sandy soils are workable but have low water-holding capacity and nutrient retention — increase plant population conservatively. Heavy clay soils can waterlog and crust; ensure good drainage and avoid compaction.

Depth: Effective rooting depth of at least 600mm is required for dryland maize. Shallower soils limit water storage and root penetration. Test for plough pans by pushing a soil probe — resistance at 200–250mm indicates compaction requiring ripping.

Drainage: Maize cannot tolerate waterlogging for more than 24–48 hours, especially during early growth. If fields have drainage problems, install sub-surface drains or plant on ridges.

Key nutrient requirements per tonne of grain: 20–25 kg N, 4–5 kg P, 4–5 kg K, plus smaller amounts of S, Zn, and B. A 6 t/ha dryland crop removes approximately 120–150 kg N, 25–30 kg P, and 25–30 kg K from the soil.

Soil preparation: Conventional: mouldboard plough to 200–250mm, followed by disc and harrow. Minimum-till: chisel plough or rip to 300mm, plant into previous season's residues. No-till: plant directly into standing stubble using a no-till planter. Conservation tillage methods reduce erosion by 60–90% and improve long-term soil health.

Planting dates by region: Mpumalanga Highveld (Ermelo, Bethal): 15 October – 30 November is the optimal window, with mid-October to mid-November being the sweet spot. Central areas (Middelburg): 20 October – 20 November. Lowveld irrigated: flexible, but avoid mid-winter frost risk. Late planting (after 15 December) reduces yield potential by 30–50%.

Planting depth: 50–75mm in moist soil. In drier conditions, plant slightly deeper (up to 80mm) to reach moisture, but never exceed 100mm. Uniform depth is critical for even emergence.

Row spacing: Dryland: 0.91m (standard) to 1.0m between rows. Irrigated: 0.76m allows higher populations and earlier canopy closure. Ultra-narrow rows (0.5m) are used in some irrigated systems but require specialised equipment.

Population targets: Dryland (500–600mm rainfall zone): 22,000–30,000 plants/ha. Dryland (600–800mm): 30,000–40,000 plants/ha. Irrigated: 60,000–80,000 plants/ha. Over-planting in dryland conditions is a common and costly mistake — rather plant conservatively in marginal areas.

Seed treatment: All commercial seed comes pre-treated with fungicides (typically metalaxyl + fludioxonil) and insecticides (imidacloprid or thiamethoxam). Additional treatment with Bradyrhizobium is not applicable to maize, but mycorrhizal seed treatments are becoming available and may improve P uptake on low-P soils.

Planter calibration: Calibrate your planter before every season. Check disc openers, seed plates/finger mechanisms, and closing wheels. Aim for less than 5% coefficient of variation in seed spacing. Uneven spacing leads to barren plants and yield loss.

Soil analysis interpretation: Always base fertilisation on a current soil analysis (not older than 3 years). Sample the 0–200mm layer, taking at least 20 cores per homogeneous land of up to 10 ha. Submit to an accredited lab (e.g., SGS, Bemlab, NviroTek, Soil Health Solutions). Your fertiliser rep or extension officer can help interpret results.

N:P:K ratios at planting: A typical recommendation for the Mpumalanga Highveld is 2:3:4(30) or 3:2:1(25) compound at 150–250 kg/ha placed in a band 50mm beside and 50mm below the seed. Never place fertiliser in direct contact with seed — salt burn will kill seedlings.

Nitrogen top-dress: Apply LAN (28% N) or urea (46% N) at 150–250 kg/ha when plants reach the 4–6 leaf stage (V4–V6, about 4 weeks after emergence). Split applications (half at V4, half at V8) improve nitrogen use efficiency, especially on sandy soils. Urea must be incorporated or applied before rain to prevent volatilisation losses.

Phosphorus: Apply all P at planting. Mpumalanga Highveld soils often have low to medium P levels. Target 15–25 mg/kg (Bray-1 method). Below 8 mg/kg, yield response to P fertilisation is dramatic.

Potassium: Most Highveld soils have adequate K, but continuous cropping without K replenishment will deplete reserves. Maintain K levels above 80 mg/kg. Sandy soils in the eastern Highveld may need K supplementation.

Micronutrients: Zinc (Zn) deficiency is common on high-pH or sandy soils — apply 5–10 kg/ha zinc sulphate or use Zn-enriched compound fertiliser. Boron (B) at 1–2 kg/ha benefits kernel set, especially under drought stress.

Lime requirements: If pH(KCl) is below 5.0, apply calcitic lime (for Ca-deficient soils) or dolomitic lime (if Mg is also low) at 1–4 t/ha. Apply 3–6 months before planting and incorporate into the top 150mm. Liming improves P availability, reduces Al toxicity, and increases microbial activity. The acid saturation method (target <10% acid saturation) is the most accurate way to calculate lime rate.

Fall armyworm (FAW — Spodoptera frugiperda): The most economically damaging pest since its arrival in SA in 2016. Larvae feed in the whorl, causing ragged leaf damage and can destroy growing points. Scout weekly from emergence — look for fresh frass in the whorl. Threshold: 10–20% of plants showing fresh damage. Registered actives include chlorantraniliprole (Coragen), emamectin benzoate, and spinetoram. Apply in late afternoon when larvae are active. Bt maize provides partial suppression but is not a standalone solution — stack with scouting and spraying when needed.

African stalk borer (Busseola fusca): Larvae bore into stalks causing "dead heart" in young plants and stalk breakage later. Apply granular insecticide (e.g., carbaryl granules) into the whorl at the 5–8 leaf stage when windowpane leaf damage is visible. Bt maize is very effective against stalk borer. Destroy crop residues after harvest and control wild grasses along field margins to reduce overwintering.

Cutworm (Agrotis spp.): Larvae cut seedlings at ground level, typically at night. Damage appears as missing plants in patches. Apply registered baits (e.g., Cutworm Bait containing carbaryl) on the soil surface in the evening. Heavy weed growth before planting harbours cutworm moths — clean plant to reduce risk.

Grey leaf spot (GLS — Cercospora zeae-maydis): The most significant foliar disease in Mpumalanga, favoured by warm, humid conditions and monoculture. Lesions are rectangular, grey-brown, and confined between leaf veins. Severe infection above the ear leaf during grain fill can reduce yields by 30–60%. Management: plant resistant/tolerant cultivars (the most cost-effective strategy), rotate with non-host crops (soybeans, sunflower), reduce residue with tillage, and consider foliar fungicide (azoxystrobin, pyraclostrobin) at tassel if susceptible cultivar and disease pressure is high.

Northern corn leaf blight (NCLB — Exserohilum turcicum): Large, cigar-shaped grey-green lesions. Worsened by continuous maize and cool, humid weather. Use resistant cultivars with Ht genes and practice crop rotation.

Common rust (Puccinia sorghi): Small, circular, reddish-brown pustules on both leaf surfaces. Usually controlled by genetic resistance. Fungicide application is rarely justified except in high-value seed production fields.

Maize streak virus (MSV): Transmitted by the leafhopper Cicadulina mbila. Causes distinctive yellow streaking on leaves, stunting, and poor cob fill. No chemical control — plant MSV-resistant cultivars (virtually all modern SA hybrids carry resistance).

Ear rot (Fusarium, Diplodia, Aspergillus): Ear rots contaminate grain with mycotoxins (fumonisins, aflatoxin). Reduce risk by: planting adapted cultivars, managing stalk borer (insect damage opens infection pathways), avoiding late-season drought stress, and harvesting promptly. Grain with visible ear rot should be tested for mycotoxins before storage or sale.

IPM strategy summary: Combine resistant cultivars, crop rotation (at least 2-year cycle with non-host crops), field scouting, economic threshold-based spraying, biological controls (Trichogramma wasps for stalk borer, naturally occurring NPV for FAW), conservation of natural enemies, and post-harvest residue management.

Moisture content targets: Harvest when grain moisture drops to 12.5–14%. Harvesting above 14% requires artificial drying, which adds cost. Harvesting below 12% increases kernel cracking and field losses. Test moisture with a calibrated grain moisture meter before starting the combine.

Combine settings: Cylinder speed: 450–600 rpm for dryland maize. Concave clearance: 15–25mm. Fan speed: adjust to blow out chaff without losing kernels. Sieve opening: 12–16mm. Walk behind the combine to check for grain in the straw — more than 1% loss indicates settings need adjustment.

Drying methods: For small volumes: sun-drying on clean concrete floors (spread 50mm deep, turn regularly, protect from rain). For larger volumes: continuous-flow dryers (drying temperature 80–100°C for grain, never exceed 110°C to avoid cracking) or batch dryers. Cool grain to within 5°C of ambient temperature after drying. Never store grain above 13% moisture — mould growth and aflatoxin contamination begin rapidly above this level.

Silo storage: Clean silos thoroughly between seasons (remove all old grain, sweep walls, treat with residual insecticide). Treat incoming grain with registered protectants such as pirimiphos-methyl (Actellic) or deltamethrin against weevils (Sitophilus zeamais) and grain borers (Prostephanus truncatus). Monitor temperature weekly — a rising temperature inside the silo indicates insect activity or moisture migration. Aerate during cool, dry weather to equalise temperatures.

Grain grading (SAGIS standards): SA maize is graded WM1, WM2, WM3 (white) or YM1, YM2, YM3 (yellow) based on defective kernels, foreign matter, moisture, and other factors. Grade WM1/YM1 achieves the best market price. Deviations (moisture above 14%, defective kernels above 5% for Grade 1, or foreign matter above 0.25%) result in grading penalties.

Expected yields: Dryland Highveld: 4–8 t/ha in a good season (long-term average ~5 t/ha). Irrigated: 10–16 t/ha with top management. Mpumalanga's 10-year average is approximately 4.5–5.5 t/ha for dryland production.

Wheat & Barley Production Guide

Wheat is South Africa's second most important grain crop after maize. It is grown as a winter crop under dryland conditions in the Western Cape and as a summer-irrigated crop in the Free State and other provinces. Barley is produced primarily for the malting industry. While Mpumalanga is not a major wheat region, irrigated wheat production is feasible on the Highveld.

Winter vs spring wheat: Winter wheat (planted May–July, harvested November–December) requires vernalisation (exposure to cold) and is grown in the Western Cape's Swartland and Southern Cape. Spring wheat (no vernalisation required) is planted under irrigation April–July in the Free State, North West, and potentially the Mpumalanga Highveld, and harvested October–December.

Dryland vs irrigated: Dryland wheat in the Western Cape relies on winter rainfall (300–500mm during the growing season). Irrigated wheat in the summer rainfall regions receives 450–600mm of supplementary irrigation over the growing season. In Mpumalanga, wheat is only viable under irrigation.

Cultivar selection: Choose cultivars listed on the ARC-Small Grain's National Cultivar Trial results for your region. Important traits: yield potential, bread-making quality (hectolitre mass, protein, falling number), disease resistance (particularly stripe rust and Russian wheat aphid), and lodging resistance. Popular irrigated cultivars include SST 806, SST 866, Pannar varieties, and Sensako selections.

Planting dates: Irrigated wheat in summer rainfall regions: mid-May to end of June (Highveld). Planting after July reduces yield potential significantly. Dryland Western Cape: mid-May to mid-June for the Swartland; April–May for the Southern Cape's longer season.

Soil requirements: pH 5.5–7.0, medium to heavy textured soils with good water-holding capacity. Wheat is more sensitive to acid soils than maize — lime if pH(KCl) is below 5.0. Effective rooting depth of 600–900mm is important for dryland production.

Planting rate: Irrigated: 80–120 kg seed/ha (targeting 200–300 plants/m²). Dryland: 50–80 kg/ha. Row spacing: 170–200mm for most planters.

Fertilisation: Irrigated: 120–180 kg N/ha (split: 40% at planting, 30% at tillering, 30% at flag leaf). P and K based on soil analysis — typically 20–30 kg P/ha and 10–20 kg K/ha at planting. Sulphur at 15–20 kg/ha benefits protein quality.

Russian wheat aphid (Diuraphis noxia): A devastating pest that injects toxic saliva causing rolled, streaked leaves and stunted heads. Plant resistant cultivars (most modern SA cultivars carry Dn resistance genes). Scout from tillering onwards; chemical control with imidacloprid or acetamiprid if populations exceed 10% of tillers infested on susceptible cultivars.

Stripe rust (Puccinia striiformis): Yellow-orange pustules in stripes along leaf veins. Can devastate susceptible cultivars in cool, moist conditions. Fungicides (triazoles, strobilurins) are effective if applied preventatively or at early infection.

Fusarium head blight (FHB — Fusarium graminearum): Bleached spikelets, shrivelled grain, mycotoxin contamination (deoxynivalenol/DON). Risk increases under warm, humid conditions during flowering. Management: resistant cultivars, avoid planting after maize (same pathogen), fungicide at flowering (tebuconazole, prothioconazole).

Harvest: At 12–13% grain moisture. Hectolitre mass (test weight) must exceed 77 kg/hl for Grade B1 bread wheat. Protein content above 12% is preferred for breadmaking. Delay in harvesting after ripening leads to sprouting risk, falling number decrease, and grade loss.

Yields: Irrigated: 6–10 t/ha with good management. Dryland Western Cape: 2–5 t/ha depending on rainfall.

Malting vs feed barley: Malting barley must meet strict quality requirements: protein 9.5–12%, germination above 95%, plump kernels (>2.5mm sieve), and no weather damage. Barley not meeting malting spec is sold as feed at a lower price. Malting contracts with South African Breweries (SAB) guarantee a market and price premium.

Cultivar selection: The South African Barley Breeding Institute (SABBI) develops cultivars suited to SA conditions. Registered malting varieties are approved by the Malting Barley Industry Association. Only plant approved cultivars if targeting malting contracts.

Growing regions: Southern Cape (dryland, winter rainfall), Western Cape Swartland, and irrigated areas in the Northern Cape (Vaalharts, Douglas). Not commonly grown in Mpumalanga due to climate, but irrigated production on the cooler Highveld is theoretically possible.

Management: Similar to wheat but with critical differences: lower nitrogen application (60–80 kg N/ha for malting — excessive N raises protein above malting spec), earlier planting (April–May), and strict harvest timing (12% moisture, no sprouting). Disease management parallels wheat — net blotch and scald are the primary diseases.

Yields: Dryland: 3–5 t/ha. Irrigated: 5–8 t/ha.

Sorghum & Other Grains

Grain sorghum is South Africa's third most important grain crop, valued for its exceptional drought tolerance. It is a key crop in drier western regions of Mpumalanga and a vital component of crop diversification strategies. Pearl millet and other indigenous grains are increasingly recognised for food security and nutrition.

Drought tolerance: Sorghum's fibrous root system can extract water from depths of 1.8m and the plant enters dormancy under severe stress, resuming growth when moisture returns. It requires only 450–650mm of rainfall during the growing season — making it viable where maize fails. Sorghum is the crop of choice for areas receiving less than 550mm rainfall.

Tannin vs non-tannin types: Tannin (bird-resistant) cultivars have a bitter seed coat that deters birds, but the tannins reduce digestibility and are unsuitable for white malt or food. Non-tannin (sweet) types are preferred for human food and malt but are highly susceptible to bird damage. Choose based on your primary market and local bird pressure.

Cultivars: ARC-recommended cultivars include PAN 8816 (non-tannin), PAN 8906, MR-Buster (tannin), and NS 5511. Hybrid sorghums outyield OPVs by 20–30%. For Mpumalanga, medium-maturity hybrids (110–120 days) are most appropriate.

Planting dates: Mid-November to mid-December for the Mpumalanga Highveld. Sorghum tolerates later planting better than maize. Soil temperature must exceed 15°C at planting depth.

Planting rate & spacing: 5–8 kg seed/ha for hybrids. Target plant population: 50,000–80,000 plants/ha for dryland, up to 120,000 irrigated. Row spacing: 0.5–0.9m. Shallow planting depth of 25–40mm — sorghum seed is small and cannot emerge from deep planting.

Fertilisation: Lower N requirements than maize: 40–80 kg N/ha for dryland (split application). P and K as per soil analysis. Sorghum responds well to residual fertility from a previous soybean or groundnut crop.

Bird damage management: Quelea birds (Quelea quelea) are the single greatest threat to sorghum in SA. They can destroy entire fields in days. Strategies: plant tannin cultivars, time planting so that the crop does not ripen when quelea populations peak (March–April), plant large blocks rather than small isolated fields, coordinate with DAFF's quelea control programme, and use bird-scaring devices. Netting is only feasible for small plots.

Harvest: At 12–14% moisture. Combine header height must be adjusted for sorghum's short stature. Yield: dryland 2–5 t/ha; irrigated 5–8 t/ha.

Markets: Animal feed (primary market), traditional opaque beer and malt (SA Breweries sources sorghum for traditional beer brands), food processing (sorghum meal, gluten-free products), and ethanol production. GrainSA facilitates market access.

Drought tolerance: Pearl millet (Pennisetum glaucum) is the most drought-tolerant cereal grain, capable of producing a crop on as little as 250–350mm rainfall. It thrives in temperatures of 25–35°C and tolerates poor, sandy soils where other crops fail.

Traditional crop: Widely grown by subsistence farmers in Limpopo, North West, and parts of Mpumalanga. Known locally as babala or nyalothi. Pearl millet is deeply embedded in African food culture.

Nutrition: Higher in protein (11–14%), iron, and zinc than maize or sorghum. Gluten-free. Excellent for food security — can be milled into flour, cooked as porridge, or malted for traditional beverages.

Planting: December–January for late-season planting. Seed rate: 3–5 kg/ha. Very shallow planting (15–25mm). Responds to modest fertilisation (30–40 kg N/ha) but produces even on unfertilised soil.

Subsistence farming: Ideal for smallholder farmers with limited resources. Low input costs, minimal pest pressure, and reliable yield under harsh conditions make it an excellent food security crop. ARC and DAFF extension services promote pearl millet as part of crop diversification for dryland farmers.

Groundnuts are covered in detail under the Oilseeds & Legumes section. They are included here as an important rotation crop in sorghum-based systems, fixing 80–120 kg N/ha for the following grain crop while producing a high-value oilseed or confectionery product.

Oilseeds & Legumes Production Guide

Soybeans, sunflower, groundnuts, dry beans, and canola are critical rotation crops that improve soil health, break disease cycles, and provide high-value products. Mpumalanga is South Africa's leading soybean producer, and the province plays an important role in sunflower and dry bean production.

Cultivar groups for Mpumalanga: Soybeans are classified by maturity group (MG). The Mpumalanga Highveld suits MG 5–6 cultivars (medium maturity, 120–140 days). Shorter-season MG 4 cultivars can be used for late planting. Select cultivars from the ARC/Protein Research Foundation national cultivar trial data — prioritise yield stability, Sclerotinia tolerance, and shattering resistance.

Inoculation with Bradyrhizobium japonicum: Essential for first-time soybean fields. Apply commercial inoculant (peat-based or liquid) directly to seed just before planting, keeping seed out of direct sunlight. Inoculation enables biological nitrogen fixation of 100–250 kg N/ha — worth R3,000–R8,000/ha in fertiliser savings. Even on previously inoculated fields, re-inoculation is recommended for maximum nodulation.

Planting dates & density: Optimal: 15 October – 30 November for the Mpumalanga Highveld. Planting after mid-December significantly reduces yield. Target 300,000–400,000 plants/ha (approximately 60–80 kg seed/ha depending on seed size). Row spacing: 0.45–0.76m; narrower rows improve canopy closure and weed suppression.

Nitrogen fixation: Well-inoculated soybeans with good nodulation (pink/red nodules when cut open) fix all their N needs from the atmosphere. No N fertiliser is required if inoculation is successful. Apply 15–20 kg P/ha and K as per soil analysis at planting.

Weed management: Critical in the first 6 weeks before canopy closure. Pre-emergence: metolachlor + metribuzin or flumioxazin. Post-emergence (Roundup Ready cultivars): glyphosate at V3–V5 stage. For non-RR cultivars: imazethapyr (Hammer), fomesafen (Flex). Volunteer maize from previous crop must be controlled with a grass-selective herbicide.

Diseases: Sclerotinia stem rot (Sclerotinia sclerotiorum) is the most damaging disease in Mpumalanga — favoured by dense canopy, irrigation, and cool wet weather during flowering. Soybean rust (Phakopsora pachyrhizi) can cause 50–80% yield loss if untreated — scout from flowering and apply fungicide (triazole + strobilurin) preventatively in high-risk seasons. Phytophthora root rot: use tolerant cultivars and avoid waterlogged fields.

Harvest: When pods are brown-dry and seeds rattle inside, typically March–May. Harvest at 13–14% moisture. Combine settings: low cylinder speed (400–500 rpm), wide concave clearance to prevent seed cracking. Soybean grade is based on moisture, damaged kernels, foreign matter, and oil content.

Markets: Oilseed crushing (soybean oil + soybean oilcake for animal feed — SA's largest protein source for poultry and livestock), full-fat soy for feed, and food-grade soybeans for tofu and soy milk. South Africa currently imports soybean oilcake, so domestic production is strategically important.

Yields: Dryland Mpumalanga: 1.5–3.0 t/ha. Irrigated: 3.0–5.0 t/ha. Top farmers achieve above 3.5 t/ha dryland.

Cultivar selection: Oil-type hybrids (standard linoleic or high-oleic — high-oleic commands a premium for cooking oil) and confectionery (large-seeded for snack market). All commercial cultivars are hybrids. Select from ARC cultivar trial results for your region. Clearfield (CL) and ExpressSun (ES) cultivars allow post-emergence herbicide application for improved weed control.

Drought tolerance: Sunflower's deep taproot (up to 2m) extracts moisture from deep in the profile. It requires only 400–600mm of rainfall and tolerates planting as late as mid-January in emergency situations. An excellent crop for drier western Mpumalanga and late-planting scenarios.

Planting: Optimal: November–December for Mpumalanga. Seeding rate: 4–5 kg/ha. Target population: 30,000–45,000 plants/ha dryland, 50,000–65,000 irrigated. Row spacing: 0.9–1.0m. Planting depth: 40–60mm.

Fertilisation: Lower N requirements than maize: 40–60 kg N/ha for dryland. All P at planting (20–30 kg P/ha). Boron is important for sunflower — deficiency causes hollow, deformed seed heads. Apply 1–2 kg B/ha as foliar spray at star-bud stage if soil B is below 0.5 mg/kg.

Pollination: Sunflower benefits greatly from bee pollination. Place beehives (2–3 hives per hectare) at the field edge during flowering to improve seed set and oil content by 10–20%. Avoid applying insecticides during flowering to protect pollinators.

Sclerotinia management (Sclerotinia sclerotiorum): Head rot and basal stem rot are the most serious diseases. The pathogen survives as sclerotia in soil for up to 8 years. Management: rotate with non-host crops (maize, wheat, sorghum — NOT soybeans, dry beans, or canola which are also hosts), maintain plant populations within recommended ranges, and apply registered fungicides at early flowering in high-risk situations.

Harvest: When the back of the head turns brown and seeds are firm. Moisture should be below 10% for safe storage (sunflower has higher oil content so lower moisture tolerance than maize). Yield: dryland 1.0–2.5 t/ha; irrigated 2.5–4.0 t/ha. Oil content: 38–45% for oil-type cultivars.

SA varieties: The ARC-Grain Crops Institute (ARC-GCI) is the primary breeder. Virginia types (large-seeded, longer season 140–160 days): Kwarts, Akwa, Anel — grown for the confectionery/in-shell market. Spanish types (smaller, shorter season 110–130 days): Harts, Jasper, Norden — used for peanut butter and oil extraction. Choose based on market and season length.

Growing regions: North West (Vaalharts), Free State, and parts of Mpumalanga (warmer Highveld and Lowveld). Groundnuts prefer warm conditions (25–30°C optimum) and light, well-drained sandy to sandy-loam soils with pH 5.8–6.5.

Planting: October–November. Depth: 50–70mm. Virginia types: 90,000–130,000 plants/ha in 0.9m rows. Spanish types: 130,000–180,000 plants/ha. Seed must be treated with registered fungicide (thiram, metalaxyl) and inoculated with Bradyrhizobium specific to groundnuts.

Calcium requirement: Groundnuts have a unique calcium requirement — the developing pegs and pods absorb Ca directly from the soil. Apply gypsum (agricultural calcium sulphate) at 400–800 kg/ha at flowering, banded over the row, especially on sandy soils with low Ca. Low Ca causes empty pods ("pops") and reduced quality.

Aflatoxin management: Aflatoxin (Aspergillus flavus/parasiticus) contamination is the most serious quality risk. Aflatoxins are carcinogenic and strictly regulated — export limits are 4 µg/kg total aflatoxin. Prevention: avoid drought stress during pod fill (irrigate if possible), harvest promptly at maturity (do not leave in the field), dry to below 8% moisture within 48 hours of lifting, and store in cool, dry conditions. Never mix damaged or discoloured kernels with sound kernels.

Harvest: Lift when 70–80% of inner pod surfaces show dark discolouration (maturity indicator). Windrow to dry in the field for 3–7 days (weather permitting) before threshing. Yield: dryland 1.0–2.5 t/ha; irrigated 3.0–5.0 t/ha (unshelled).

Types grown in SA: Sugar beans (red speckled — most popular in SA, accounting for ~40% of production), small white (canning and baked beans), red speckled (large), large white kidney (export to Europe/Middle East). The dry bean market is dominated by sugar beans, which command the highest domestic prices.

Growing conditions: Warm-season crop, 18–24°C optimum. Sensitive to frost, waterlogging, and extreme heat during flowering. Suited to the warmer Mpumalanga Highveld (Middelburg, Belfast corridor) and Limpopo.

Planting: November–December. Row spacing: 0.5–0.76m. Population: 150,000–250,000 plants/ha depending on type and growth habit (bush vs semi-climbing). Inoculate with Rhizobium phaseoli for nitrogen fixation.

Disease management: Common bacterial blight, angular leaf spot, anthracnose, and rust are the main diseases. Use certified disease-free seed, rotate with non-legume crops for at least 2 years, and apply preventive fungicides (mancozeb, azoxystrobin) from flowering. Sclerotinia white mould is devastating under cool, wet conditions — manage with wider spacing, fungicide, and crop rotation.

Harvest: When pods are dry and seeds rattle (14–16% moisture). Swath or desiccate with paraquat to speed drying if weather threatens. Thresh gently — beans are easily damaged. Grade based on seed size, colour, defects, and moisture. Yield: 1.0–2.5 t/ha dryland.

Markets: Domestic consumption (canning, retail packs), export (large white kidney beans to Europe, sugar beans to East Africa). Dry beans are a high-value crop per hectare, especially sugar beans, making them attractive for smallholder and emerging farmers.

Winter crop: Canola (Brassica napus) is a winter oilseed crop grown almost exclusively in the Western Cape under dryland conditions (planted April–May, harvested October–November). It is a key rotation crop with wheat, breaking disease cycles and improving soil structure.

Potential in cooler Mpumalanga areas: While not commercially established in Mpumalanga, canola could theoretically be grown in cool, high-altitude areas (above 1,600m) with supplemental irrigation during winter. Research trials would be needed to validate cultivar adaptation, as current SA cultivars are selected for Mediterranean-type winter rainfall climates.

Agronomics: Canola requires well-drained soils with pH above 5.5 (very sensitive to acid soils and Al toxicity). Seeding rate: 3–5 kg/ha at 5–10mm depth. Very small seed — seedbed preparation must be fine and firm. Nitrogen requirement: 80–120 kg N/ha. Sulphur at 20–30 kg/ha is essential for oil quality.

Key pests: Diamondback moth (same pest as on cabbage), aphids, and bollworm. Sclerotinia is a major disease (same pathogen affecting soybeans and sunflower).

Harvest: At 8–10% moisture. Canola shatters easily, so timing is critical. Windrow at 40–60% seed colour change or direct combine with a pod sealant. Oil content: 40–45%. Yield: 1.5–3.0 t/ha dryland Western Cape.

Vegetable Production Guide

Mpumalanga's diverse climate zones — from the subtropical Lowveld to the cool Highveld — support year-round vegetable production. The province produces fresh vegetables for Johannesburg's city deep market and national retailers. Vegetables are high-value, quick-turnover crops ideal for both large commercial and smallholder farmers.

Varieties: Determinate (bush type, e.g., Heinz, Star 9003, Rodade — suited to open-field processing) vs indeterminate (staked/trellised, continuous growth — Endeavour, Christiaan, Pakmor). Roma/plum types for processing and paste; round types for fresh market; cherry/cocktail for premium retail.

Greenhouse vs open field: Greenhouse production (poly-tunnels or shade net) allows year-round production, better quality, and significantly reduced disease pressure. Open-field production is lower cost but higher risk. In Mpumalanga's Lowveld, open-field production peaks in winter (April–September, frost-free); Highveld production is summer-focused (September–March) or under protection.

Soil: Deep, well-drained loam or sandy-loam with pH 6.0–6.8. Add 15–25 t/ha of well-composted kraal manure before transplanting. Tomatoes are very sensitive to waterlogging — raised beds improve drainage in heavy soils.

Spacing: Open field determinate: 1.2–1.5m between rows, 0.3–0.5m in-row (25,000–30,000 plants/ha). Indeterminate staked: 1.5m rows, 0.4–0.5m in-row.

Irrigation: Drip irrigation is strongly recommended — delivers water directly to roots, reduces foliar wetness and disease. Water requirement: 4–7mm/day during peak growth (fruiting stage). Total seasonal requirement: 500–800mm. Irregular watering causes blossom end rot (calcium deficiency exacerbated by water stress).

Fertilisation: Heavy feeders. Pre-plant: 800–1,200 kg/ha of 2:3:4(30). Fertigation through drip: weekly applications of calcium nitrate (150 kg/ha/season), potassium nitrate, and MAP. Total N requirement: 180–250 kg/ha over the season.

Bacterial wilt (Ralstonia solanacearum): Devastating soilborne disease — plants wilt and die rapidly. No effective chemical control. Manage by: crop rotation with non-solanaceous crops for 5+ years, grafting onto resistant rootstocks (becoming standard practice), using pathogen-free transplants, and improving soil drainage.

Early blight (Alternaria solani) & late blight (Phytophthora infestans): Early blight causes concentric-ring leaf spots; late blight causes water-soaked lesions, rapid defoliation, and fruit rot. Preventive fungicide programme: mancozeb alternated with metalaxyl (for late blight) or azoxystrobin, on a 7–10 day schedule during wet weather. Remove infected plant debris promptly.

Root-knot nematodes (Meloidogyne spp.): Cause root galling, stunting, and wilt. Extremely common in sandy Lowveld soils. Manage with crop rotation (include nematode-reducing crops like mustard or sorghum), resistant rootstocks, and soil fumigation or biological nematicides (Paecilomyces, Trichoderma) where severe.

Harvest: Pick at "breaker" to "turning" stage for distant markets (7–10 day shelf life) or "light red" for local markets. Handle carefully — tomatoes bruise easily. Yield: open field 40–80 t/ha; greenhouse 100–200+ t/ha over an extended season.

Planting times: Cool-season crops. In Mpumalanga: transplant February–May for winter/spring harvest on the Highveld; year-round in cooler microclimates. Lowveld: April–July only (too hot in summer). Optimal growing temperature: 15–20°C. Cabbage tolerates light frost; cauliflower and broccoli are more sensitive to temperature extremes.

Varieties: Cabbage — Star 3301, Tenacity, Grand Slam (disease resistant, high-yielding, marketable head size). Cauliflower — Snow Crown, Fremont. Broccoli — Marathon, Green Magic. Choose varieties with appropriate maturity (60–100 days from transplant) and heat tolerance for your production window.

Spacing: Cabbage: 0.6m x 0.4m (40,000 plants/ha) for medium-head fresh market; wider for large heads. Cauliflower/broccoli: 0.6m x 0.45m. Transplant 4–6 week old seedlings with 4–5 true leaves.

Fertilisation: Heavy feeders requiring 150–200 kg N/ha, 40–60 kg P/ha, and 100–150 kg K/ha over the season. Base dressing: 600–800 kg/ha of 2:3:4(30). Top-dress with LAN at 3 and 6 weeks after transplanting. Boron and molybdenum are critical micronutrients — deficiency causes hollow stem (B) and whiptail (Mo).

Diamondback moth (Plutella xylostella): The most serious brassica pest worldwide, with confirmed resistance to many insecticide groups in SA. IPM approach: use Bt insecticides (Bacillus thuringiensis var. kurstaki) as the backbone, rotate with spinosad and chlorantraniliprole, scout twice weekly, conserve natural enemies (parasitic wasps), and time planting to avoid peak moth populations.

Black rot (Xanthomonas campestris): Bacterial disease causing V-shaped yellow lesions from leaf margins and blackened veins. Spreads rapidly in warm, wet conditions. Use hot-water-treated seed (50°C for 30 minutes), resistant varieties, and 2–3 year crop rotation away from all brassicas.

Harvest: Cabbage: when heads are firm and solid (test by pressing — should feel dense). Cauliflower: when curds are compact, white, and 15–20cm diameter (before riciness develops). Broccoli: when central head is tight and dark green, before florets begin to open. Yield: cabbage 40–80 t/ha; cauliflower/broccoli 15–25 t/ha.

Swiss chard (Beta vulgaris var. cicla): The most widely grown "spinach" in South Africa. Extremely productive, heat tolerant, and easy to grow. Varieties: Ford Hook Giant, Lucullus, Bright Lights (coloured stems for premium markets). Sow year-round in mild areas; avoid extreme cold. Spacing: 30cm x 40cm. Begin harvesting outer leaves 6–8 weeks after sowing; continues producing for 3–6 months with regular harvesting and fertilisation.

Baby spinach (Spinacia oleracea): True spinach — a cool-season crop for premium markets. Grow in autumn/winter in Mpumalanga (March–August). Sow densely (100–150 seeds/m² in beds). Harvest entire plant at 25–35 days for baby leaf. Very sensitive to heat and long days (bolts quickly in summer). Requires well-drained, fertile soil with pH 6.5–7.5.

Amaranth/morogo (Amaranthus spp.): Traditional indigenous leafy green of enormous nutritional value — higher in protein, calcium, iron, and vitamin A than cabbage or spinach. Grows vigorously in warm conditions (October–March). Tolerates poor soils and drought. Known as thepe, imbuya, or vowa depending on region. Increasingly sold in formal retail markets as demand for indigenous vegetables grows.

Lettuce: Types: iceberg (head, for burger/fast food industry), cos/romaine, butterhead, and loose-leaf (red and green). Cool-season crop, best for Highveld autumn/winter production. Very sensitive to heat — bolts above 25°C. Head lettuce: 30cm x 30cm spacing. High-value crop for direct sales to restaurants and retailers.

Nutrition & fertilisation: All leafy greens are nitrogen-hungry. Apply LAN (28% N) or urea as side-dressing every 2–3 weeks at 30–50 kg N/ha per application. Foliar feeding with liquid seaweed or complete micronutrient solution boosts growth and leaf quality. Excessive nitrogen on spinach and lettuce can increase nitrate levels in leaves — follow recommended rates.

Planting seasons summary: Swiss chard: year-round. Baby spinach: March–August. Amaranth: October–March. Lettuce: March–September (Highveld). All: protect from frost with row cover or plastic tunnels for extended seasons.

Certified seed: Always use certified seed potatoes from a registered seed grower (Potatoes SA seed certification scheme). Certified seed is inspected for viruses, bacterial wilt, and blackleg. Farm-saved seed accumulates diseases over generations and leads to declining yields — a false economy.

Cultivars: Mondial (most widely planted in SA — high yield, good processing quality, smooth skin), Sifra (excellent fresh market appearance, white flesh), Valor (broad adaptation, disease tolerance), BP1 (traditional SA favourite, early maturing, excellent taste but susceptible to common scab), Lanorma, Innovator (French fry industry), and FL2108 (new, gaining popularity). Cultivar choice depends on target market (fresh, chips, crisps, processing).

Planting depth & spacing: Plant certified seed tubers (50–80g, or cut larger tubers ensuring at least 2 eyes per piece) at 100–150mm depth in ridged rows. Row spacing: 0.75–0.9m. In-row spacing: 0.25–0.35m (targeting 35,000–55,000 plants/ha). Wider spacing produces larger tubers; closer spacing increases total number of tubers.

Soil & preparation: Loose, well-drained sandy-loam or loam with pH 5.0–6.5. Potatoes prefer slightly acid soils — high pH increases common scab incidence. Deep tillage (ripping to 400mm) followed by rotovation to create a fine, loose seedbed free of clods and compaction. Good ridge formation is essential for tuber development and to prevent greening.

Irrigation: Potatoes are sensitive to water stress — irregular watering causes growth cracks, hollow heart, and second growth. Total water requirement: 500–700mm per season. Centre-pivot or drip irrigation. Critical period: tuber initiation through bulking (40–90 days after planting). Gradually reduce irrigation 2 weeks before harvest.

Late blight (Phytophthora infestans): The most destructive potato disease worldwide. Water-soaked lesions on leaves, white sporulation on undersides, and rapid defoliation in wet weather. Can destroy a crop within 2 weeks. Preventive fungicide programme is non-negotiable: apply mancozeb + cymoxanil or mandipropamid on a 5–7 day schedule during wet conditions. Resistance monitoring is important — new strains appear regularly.

Other diseases: Early blight (Alternaria solani), common scab (Streptomyces scabies — manage with acid soil, irrigation during tuber set), blackleg/soft rot (Pectobacterium — use certified seed, avoid mechanical damage).

Harvesting & grading: Destroy haulms (chemical desiccation with diquat or mechanical flailing) 2–3 weeks before harvest to allow skin set. Harvest when soil is dry to reduce mechanical damage. Grade according to Potatoes SA standards: Class 1, 2, 3 based on size, shape, defects, greening, and mechanical damage. Cold-store at 4–8°C and 85–90% humidity for fresh market; 8–10°C for processing potatoes (lower sugar development).

Yields: Irrigated: 40–65 t/ha for good management. Top farms exceed 70 t/ha. Dryland: 15–30 t/ha depending on rainfall.

Onion day-length types: SA grows short-day (10–12 hour) and intermediate-day (12–14 hour) onion varieties. Short-day types (e.g., Texas Grano, Star 6003) are planted in March–April for winter growth and October–November harvest. Intermediate types (e.g., Caledon, Dorado) are suited to slightly longer photoperiods. Long-day types (European) are not suitable for SA latitudes.

Planting dates for Mpumalanga: Transplant onion seedlings March–May for Highveld production. Direct seeding: February–March. In the warmer Lowveld, planting shifts earlier (February–March). Onions require 120–150 days from transplant to harvest.

Soil & fertilisation: Well-drained sandy-loam, pH 6.0–7.0. NPK at planting: 120 kg N, 40 kg P, 60 kg K per hectare. Side-dress N at 4 and 8 weeks. Sulphur (20–30 kg S/ha) improves pungency and storage quality.

Curing & storage: After lifting, cure onions in a well-ventilated space for 7–14 days until neck is completely dry and outer skins are papery. Store at 0–4°C and 65–70% relative humidity for up to 6 months (hard, pungent varieties store best). Poor curing leads to neck rot and short shelf life.

Garlic: Plant individual cloves March–May, 50mm deep, 100mm apart in rows 300mm apart. Garlic requires a cold period for bulb formation. Harvest when 50% of leaves have yellowed (September–November). Cure for 2–3 weeks. SA garlic market is dominated by imports; local production is a niche high-value opportunity.

Carrots: Deep, stone-free sandy-loam soil is essential — any obstruction causes forked or deformed roots. pH 6.0–7.0. Direct seed at 1–2 million seeds/ha for baby carrots, 500,000–800,000 for fresh market. Sow March–July for Mpumalanga. Key pest: carrot fly. Varieties: Kuroda types (heat tolerant), Nantes types (premium fresh market).

Beetroot: Hardy, cold-tolerant root vegetable. Sow March–August. Spacing: 250mm x 80mm. Harvest at 60–80mm diameter (60–90 days). Popular for fresh market, baby beet for premium retail, and processing. Varieties: Detroit Dark Red, Crimson Globe.

Butternut (Cucurbita moschata): South Africa's most important cucurbit. Warm-season crop requiring 20–30°C. Plant October–December in Mpumalanga. Spacing: 2.0m x 0.5m (single rows) or 3.0m x 0.5m for trailing types. Popular varieties: Waltham, Atlas, Star 6001. Yield: 15–30 t/ha. Stores well for 2–3 months at ambient temperature — excellent for food security.

Pumpkin: Boer pumpkin (Flat White Boer) is a SA staple. Extremely vigorous — space plants 3–4m apart. Plant October–December. Harvest when stem dries and skin is hard. Yield: 20–50 t/ha. Also used for animal feed.

Watermelon: Requires warm conditions (25–30°C), long growing season (80–110 days), and sandy soil. Plant November–December in Mpumalanga. Pollination by bees is essential for fruit set. Varieties: Crimson Sweet, Sugar Baby, seedless hybrids (require a pollinator variety planted at 1:3 ratio). Harvest when ground spot turns yellow and tendril nearest fruit dries.

Gem squash: Compact plants suited to smaller gardens and intensive production. Plant October–January. Space 1.0m x 0.5m. Harvest at 60–80 days when skin is hard and dark green. Very popular in SA fresh market.

Pollination: All cucurbits depend on insect pollination (primarily bees). Poor pollination causes misshapen, small, or aborted fruit. Maintain bee-friendly practices — avoid insecticide sprays during morning flowering hours. Place hives near fields for commercial production.

Powdery mildew: The most common cucurbit disease — white powdery patches on leaves, reducing photosynthesis and yield. Manage with resistant varieties, sulphur-based fungicides or newer actives (myclobutanil, trifloxystrobin), and good air circulation. Remove severely infected foliage.

Sweet peppers (bell peppers): High-value crop for fresh market and export. Greenhouse production dominates for quality. Varieties: red, yellow, green, and orange blocky types (e.g., Ferrari, Celica, Magno). Open-field production is possible in frost-free areas. Plant October–November (open field) or year-round (greenhouse). Spacing: 0.4m x 0.3m under protection. Yield: open field 20–40 t/ha; greenhouse 60–120 t/ha.

Hot pepper/chilli cultivars: Jalapeno (processing for sauces), Cayenne (drying), Habanero (very hot, niche market), Serrano, Thai chilli, Bird's eye/peri-peri (African Bird's Eye — wild-harvested and cultivated, high export demand for peri-peri sauce industry). Growing demand for locally produced chillies driven by the hot sauce market.

Growing conditions: All Capsicum species require warm conditions (20–30°C), full sun, and well-drained fertile soil with pH 6.0–6.8. Frost-free growing season of at least 120 days. In Mpumalanga: Lowveld is ideal year-round; Highveld requires transplanting after frost risk passes (October) or greenhouse production.

Eggplant/brinjal (Solanum melongena): Warm-season crop requiring 25–35°C. More heat-tolerant than tomatoes or peppers. Plant October–November on the Highveld. Spacing: 0.8m x 0.5m. Varieties: Black Beauty (classic dark purple), Long Purple, white and green types for Indian cuisine market. Similar disease profile to tomatoes — manage bacterial wilt, Verticillium wilt, and spider mites.

Common management practices: All solanaceous vegetables benefit from staking/trellising (improved air circulation, easier harvesting), drip irrigation, mulching (reduces weed pressure and conserves moisture), and crop rotation with non-solanaceous crops for at least 3 years to break disease cycles.

Fruit Production Guide

South Africa is a major global fruit exporter. Mpumalanga's subtropical Lowveld (Nelspruit/Mbombela, White River, Hazyview, Komatipoort, Malelane, Barberton) is one of the country's premier fruit-producing regions for citrus, avocados, mangoes, bananas, litchis, and macadamia nuts. The cooler Highveld offers limited potential for deciduous fruit and berries.

Types grown in SA: Valencia oranges (SA's largest citrus export — harvested May–October), Navel oranges (March–July), Lemons (year-round, Eureka and Lisbon), Grapefruit (Star Ruby for red, Marsh for white — Mpumalanga/Limpopo produce most of SA's grapefruit), and Soft citrus/mandarins (Nadorcott, Orri, Satsuma — fastest growing category in SA citrus, driven by global demand for easy-peelers).

Rootstocks: Rootstock selection determines tree size, yield, fruit quality, and disease tolerance. Rough lemon (vigorous, drought tolerant, but susceptible to Phytophthora — widely used in Mpumalanga), Carrizo/Troyer citrange (smaller trees, better fruit quality, CTV tolerant), Swingle citrumelo (cold tolerant, Phytophthora tolerant, suited to replant situations). Consult CRI guidelines for rootstock-scion compatibility for your specific location and soil type.

CRI (Citrus Research International) guidelines: CRI is the industry's research body. Follow their recommendations on cultivar selection, pest monitoring programmes, maximum residue level (MRL) compliance for export markets, cold sterilisation protocols, and phytosanitary requirements. CRI Cutting Edge newsletters provide regular updated technical information.

HLB/Citrus greening threat: Huanglongbing (HLB) is the world's most devastating citrus disease, caused by Candidatus Liberibacter bacteria transmitted by the Asian citrus psyllid (Diaphorina citri). SA is currently free of the Asian psyllid but has the African citrus psyllid (Trioza erytreae) which transmits the African form of greening. The African form is less severe but still damaging. If you suspect HLB (blotchy mottle on leaves, lopsided fruit with aborted seeds, twig dieback), report immediately to CRI or DALRRD. Early detection is critical for containment.

False codling moth (FCM — Thaumatodea leucotreta): SA's most important citrus pest for export. Larvae bore into fruit causing internal damage and pre-harvest drop. FCM presence triggers phytosanitary rejections in EU and other markets. IPM programme: sterile insect technique (SIT — CRI programme releasing sterile moths), mating disruption, attract-and-kill stations, orchard sanitation (collect and destroy all dropped fruit), and targeted insecticide sprays (codling moth granulosis virus, spinetoram). A systems approach combining multiple methods is required for export compliance.

Packhouse standards & export protocols: Export citrus must comply with GlobalGAP certification, HACCP food safety standards, and specific phytosanitary requirements of the destination market (EU, USA, China, Japan each have different protocols). Cold sterilisation treatments (-0.6°C for 22 days for certain markets) are required for some pest-market combinations. Grade and size standards determine the packing fraction and revenue per hectare.

Irrigation: Micro-sprinkler or drip. Young trees: 30–60 L/week. Mature trees: 120–200 L/week during summer, adjusted by ET-based scheduling. Total annual water requirement: 800–1,200mm. Regulated deficit irrigation during Stage II fruit development can improve fruit quality.

Yields: Mature Valencia orchards: 50–80 t/ha. Navels: 40–60 t/ha. Soft citrus: 30–50 t/ha. Lemons: 40–60 t/ha.

Avocados (Hass, Fuerte, Ryan): Mpumalanga (Hazyview, White River, Tzaneen corridor) is SA's largest avocado-producing region. Hass dominates (~65% of production) for export — dark, pebbly skin, excellent flavour, long shelf life. Fuerte (green-skinned, smooth) is an older variety with declining market share. Ryan is a late-season Hass-type. Maluma (SA-bred) is gaining popularity.

Avocado Phytophthora management: Phytophthora cinnamomi root rot is the #1 disease threat. Avocados are extremely susceptible to waterlogging. Management (per SUBTROP guidelines): plant on ridges or mounds for drainage, mulch with coarse organic material (15–20cm of wood chips), apply phosphonate (potassium phosphite) as trunk injections or soil drench 2–3 times per year, and ensure irrigation does not over-wet the root zone. Healthy organic mulch harbours antagonistic microorganisms that suppress Phytophthora.

SUBTROP guidelines: SUBTROP is the industry body for avocado, mango, and litchi. Their best practice guidelines cover orchard establishment, nutrition (avocados need high potassium, 1.5–2.0% K in leaf analysis), pruning for light penetration, pollination (avocados have complex A/B flowering types — interplant compatible types for cross-pollination), and export quality standards.

Mangoes: Tommy Atkins (most commercially planted worldwide, disease resistant, good shelf life, less flavour), Kent (excellent flavour, fibreless, gaining market share), Keitt (large, late-season), Sensation (early season, local favourite). Planted in frost-free Lowveld areas. Anthracnose (Colletotrichum gloeosporioides) is the primary disease — pre-harvest and post-harvest fungicide treatments are essential for marketable fruit. Fruit fly (Ceratitis cosyra, Bactrocera dorsalis) control is mandatory for export — use protein bait sprays, MAT (Male Annihilation Technique), and orchard sanitation.

Litchis: Mauritius is the main variety (80%+ of SA production). Grown in Hazyview, Tzaneen area. Extremely sensitive to dry winters during flower induction — irrigation during June–August is critical. Harvest November–January. Post-harvest sulphur dioxide treatment prevents browning. SA exports primarily to EU.

Bananas: Cavendish group (Williams, Grand Nain) dominates. Grown in frost-free areas: Komatipoort, Malelane, Kiepersol, Levubu. Tissue culture plantlets from accredited nurseries ensure virus-free planting material. Panama disease Tropical Race 4 (TR4 — Fusarium oxydominium f.sp. cubense): TR4 was confirmed in Mozambique and poses an existential threat to SA's banana industry. There is no cure — the fungus persists in soil indefinitely. Strict biosecurity: disinfect vehicles, boots, and tools entering banana plantations. Report any suspicious wilting to DALRRD immediately.

Guavas & pawpaws/papaya: Guavas grown in Mpumalanga and Limpopo for processing (juice, canning). Pawpaw/papaya is a fast-growing tropical crop for the Lowveld, producing fruit within 9–12 months of planting. Solo-type papayas for export; larger Maradol/local types for domestic market.

Limited in Mpumalanga: Most SA deciduous fruit (apples, pears, stone fruit, table grapes) is produced in the Western Cape, which provides the winter chill hours (400–1,000+ hours below 7°C) these crops require. Mpumalanga's Highveld receives some chill, but generally insufficient for apples or pears.

Peaches & nectarines in cooler areas: Low-chill peach and nectarine varieties (requiring 200–400 chill hours) can be grown in the cooler Mpumalanga Highveld (Ermelo, Carolina, Dullstroom area). Varieties bred for low-chill requirements include selections from the ARC-Infruitec programme. Production is limited and primarily for local fresh market and farm stalls.

Table grapes potential: Early-season table grape production is possible in the warmer Lowveld under irrigation, exploiting the market window before Western Cape production begins. However, establishing a table grape vineyard requires significant capital, technical expertise, and packhouse infrastructure. Varieties such as Prime, Flame, and Crimson Seedless are suited to warm climates with short winters.

Blueberries: South Africa's blueberry industry has expanded dramatically, now ranking among the world's top 10 exporters. The industry exploits the Northern Hemisphere off-season (SA harvest: August–January) to access premium export prices. Most production is under netting or in tunnels. Low-chill "evergreen" cultivars (e.g., Ventura, Twilight, from breeding programmes by Fall Creek, Draper) are suited to Mpumalanga's Highveld. Substrate (coir/peat) production in bags is common, enabling precise pH control (4.5–5.5 — blueberries need very acid conditions). High-value crop: R200,000–R500,000+/ha gross revenue potential, but establishment costs exceed R1 million/ha.

Strawberries: Produced in cooler areas for fresh market. The SA industry is concentrated in the Western Cape and Gauteng/Mpumalanga. Day-neutral (everbearing) varieties enable extended harvest seasons. Planted in raised beds, plastic mulch, and drip irrigation. Tunnel production extends the season and improves quality. Varieties: Albion, San Andreas, Monterey. Labour-intensive crop requiring daily hand-picking.

Blackberries & raspberries: Emerging industries in SA. Thornless blackberry varieties (e.g., Loch Ness, Ouachita) are more practical for commercial picking. Raspberries are the highest-value berry per kilogram but very perishable. Both are suited to tunnel production in cooler Mpumalanga areas with protection structures. Market primarily through formal retail and restaurant channels.

Sugarcane Production Guide

Sugarcane is a major crop in the eastern Lowveld of Mpumalanga, particularly around Komatipoort, Malelane, and Barberton, as well as in KwaZulu-Natal (SA's largest producing region). The warm subtropical climate, fertile alluvial soils, and irrigation infrastructure support high yields. The SA sugar industry is regulated by the SA Sugar Association (SASA) and supported by SASRI (SA Sugarcane Research Institute).

SASRI-recommended varieties (N-series): SASRI breeds and evaluates varieties through its Variety Evaluation Programme. Current recommendations for the Mpumalanga Lowveld irrigated region include N12 (widely planted, reliable), N31, N36, N41 (excellent ratooning, high sucrose), N49, N53 (good early season), N57, N58, and N59. Each variety has specific characteristics for soil type, harvest period, and disease profile — consult the SASRI Variety Information Sheets for your mill area.

Planting methods: Conventional: plant whole stalks or setts (3-budded cuttings) in furrows at 1.2–1.5m row spacing. Trench planting: deeper furrows (200–300mm) for better ratoon establishment. Spaced transplanting: emerging from SASRI research, using single-budded setts in trays for more uniform plant stands and reduced seed cane requirement. Hot water treatment (50°C for 2 hours) of seed cane before planting is essential to control ratoon stunting disease (RSD).

Row spacing: Standard: 1.2m for irrigated, 1.4–1.5m for dryland/rainfed. Wider spacing allows better air circulation and mechanical access but reduces canopy closure speed. Dual-row configurations (0.5m twin rows on 1.5m centres) are used in some areas to maximise yield.

Ratoon management: Sugarcane is harvested as ratoons (regrowth from root system) for 6–10 cycles before replanting, making it one of the most labour-efficient crops per unit output. Good ratoon management: cut cane close to ground level, avoid stool damage by machinery, retain trash as mulch (green cane harvesting reduces erosion and conserves moisture), fertilise promptly after harvest, and manage weeds early in the ratoon cycle. Ratoon yield typically declines 5–10% per cycle — replant when ratoon yield drops below 80% of the plant crop.

Water requirements: Sugarcane requires 1,200–1,800mm of water per growth cycle (12–24 months depending on region and management). In the Mpumalanga Lowveld, rainfall provides 500–700mm and the balance must be supplied by irrigation. Peak water demand (8–10mm/day) occurs during the grand growth phase (active tillering and stalk elongation, 4–10 months after planting).

Irrigation methods: Furrow irrigation (traditional, 50–60% efficient but simple infrastructure), overhead/centre-pivot sprinklers (70–80% efficient, good for undulating topography), drip irrigation (90–95% efficient, highest water saving, ideal for Lowveld water-limited areas, but higher capital and maintenance cost), and dragline sprinklers (common in KZN and Lowveld smallholder operations).

Irrigation scheduling: Use SASRI's MyCaneSim model, soil moisture probes (capacitance or tensiometers), or the A-pan evaporation method (apply water when cumulative crop evapotranspiration reaches a set depletion level, typically 50% of available soil water). Over-irrigation wastes water, leaches nutrients, and promotes waterlogging and root disease.

Fertigation: Applying fertiliser through drip irrigation systems (fertigation) improves nutrient use efficiency by 20–30%. Dissolve urea, MAP, or KCl in irrigation water and inject at scheduled intervals. SASRI provides fertigation schedules based on crop stage and soil analysis.

Eldana borer (Eldana saccharina): The most damaging sugarcane pest in SA. Larvae bore into stalks, destroying fibre and allowing secondary infection by red rot fungi. Yield losses of 0.5–1.5 t sucrose/ha are common in affected fields. IPM: harvest sugarcane before 18 months old (Eldana damage increases exponentially with crop age), use resistant varieties (N-series varieties have varying Eldana tolerance ratings), push-pull strategies using trap crops, biological control with Trichogramma egg parasitoids (SASRI releases 150,000+ wasps/ha), and pheromone monitoring traps. Green cane harvesting (leaving trash blanket) reduces Eldana overwintering compared to burn-harvest.

Yellow sugarcane aphid (Sipha flava): Feeds on leaves causing yellowing, stunting, and honeydew/sooty mould. Infestations are worst during dry spells. Resistant varieties are the primary defence; chemical control is rarely justified as natural enemies (ladybirds, lacewings, parasitoids) usually achieve biological control within 2–3 weeks.

Smut (Sporisorium scitamineum): Produces a characteristic black whip-like structure emerging from the growing point. Infected stools produce thin, whippy stalks with no commercial value. Management: plant resistant varieties, rogue out (remove and destroy) infected stools, and use hot-water-treated seed cane to eliminate seed-borne smut.

Ratoon Stunting Disease (RSD — Leifsonia xyli subsp. xyli): Bacterial disease causing progressive stunting and yield decline of 15–30% in ratoons. No external symptoms — diagnosis requires laboratory testing (SASRI provides a free diagnostic service). Spread on contaminated cutting implements. Prevention: hot water treatment of seed cane (50°C for 2 hours, then 51°C for 30 minutes), disinfection of cane knives with 70% methylated spirits or Dettol between varieties/fields, and planting only certified seed cane from SASRI-approved nurseries.

SASRI advisory services: SASRI employs extension specialists and entomologists who provide free on-farm advisory visits to registered growers. Contact your local SASRI office or mill extension team for pest and disease identification, variety recommendations, and management advice.

Ripening: Chemical ripeners (e.g., fluazifop/Fusilade Forte, glyphosate at sub-lethal rate, or ethephon) are applied 8–14 weeks before harvest to redirect growth from stalk elongation to sucrose accumulation. Ripeners can increase recoverable sucrose by 0.5–1.5 units. Application must be carefully managed — excessive ripener or incorrect timing can damage the ratoon crop.

Harvest timing: Harvest during the peak sucrose period for your variety and region. In the Mpumalanga Lowveld, the milling season typically runs April–December. Early-season varieties (e.g., N53) are harvested April–June; mid-season (N12, N41) June–September; late-season (N36) September–December. Sucrose content is tested using a refractometer (Brix reading) on juice from stalk samples.

Harvest method: Manual cutting (with cane knives) or mechanical harvesting (chopper harvesters). Cut cane at ground level — each centimetre of stump left contains the highest sucrose concentration. Remove tops and trash unless practising green cane harvesting. Avoid soil contamination during loading — soil lowers sugar recovery at the mill.

Sucrose maximisation: Deliver cane to the mill within 24–48 hours of cutting. Sucrose deterioration (inversion to glucose and fructose by bacteria and enzymes) begins immediately after cutting and accelerates in warm weather. Delays beyond 48 hours reduce recoverable value (RV) significantly — this directly impacts your payment. Avoid leaving cut cane in the field over weekends.

Mill delivery protocols: All cane is weighed and sampled at the mill. Payment is based on Recoverable Value (RV), which measures the actual sucrose recovered per tonne of cane. Target: 10–14% RV for well-managed cane. The cane testing service is administered by SASA to ensure fair payment. Deliver clean cane — deductions are applied for excessive trash, soil, and tops.

Expected yields: Irrigated Lowveld: 80–130 t/ha cane (plant crop), 70–110 t/ha (ratoon average). Rainfed KZN: 50–80 t/ha. Sucrose yield: 8–15 t sucrose/ha for well-managed irrigated operations.

Nut Production Guide

South Africa is the world's largest producer of macadamia nuts and a significant pecan nut producer. Mpumalanga's subtropical Lowveld (White River, Hazyview, Barberton, Nelspruit) is the heartland of SA macadamia production. Nut crops are high-value, long-term investments requiring patience and capital but offering excellent returns once established.

SA is the world's largest producer: South Africa produces over 50,000 tonnes of macadamia nut-in-shell (NIS) annually, accounting for ~27% of global production. The industry is valued at over R10 billion and growing. Mpumalanga produces approximately 40% of SA's macadamia crop, with major growing areas around White River, Hazyview, Barberton, Kiepersol, and Nelspruit.

Varieties: Beaumont (vigorous, high yield, good kernel recovery — the most widely planted cultivar in SA), HAES 816 (excellent kernel quality, premium pricing, moderate yield), A4 (Nelmak 2 — bred by ARC, very high kernel recovery of 30–35%), 695 (Pahala — high yield but variable kernel quality), 344 (high quality, erratic bearing in some areas), and 788 (reliable, good pollinator). Plant at least 2–3 varieties for cross-pollination and to spread harvest timing. Select varieties based on SAMAC (SA Macadamia Association) cultivar performance data for your specific region.

Establishment: A long-term investment — trees begin light production at year 4–5, first commercial crop at year 7, and reach full bearing at year 12–15. This means no income from the orchard for 5–7 years while maintenance costs accumulate. Establishment cost: R100,000–R200,000/ha for trees, irrigation, and infrastructure. Space trees 8m x 4m (312 trees/ha) for high-density systems or 10m x 5m (200 trees/ha) for conventional spacing.

Growing conditions: Altitude 300–800m asl, mean annual temperature 18–25°C, rainfall 1,000–1,500mm (or irrigation to supplement), frost-free. Deep, well-drained acid soils (pH 5.0–6.5). Wind protection is critical — macadamia branches are brittle and break in strong winds. Plant windbreaks (Casuarina, Grevillea) before or simultaneously with the orchard.

Stink bug IPM: Two-spotted stink bug (Bathycoelia distincta) and green vegetable bug (Nezara viridula) are the most damaging macadamia pests. They pierce the shell and feed on the developing kernel, causing brown spots, internal damage, and reject-quality nuts. Damage is invisible externally — it only appears after cracking, causing major quality losses. IPM programme: regular monitoring with knock-down sheets (10 trees per block, weekly), threshold-based spraying (0.5 bugs per tree triggers action), trunk treatments with systemic insecticides, trap crops (soybeans planted as a trap along orchard borders), and habitat management to encourage predatory wasps and birds.

Nut quality: Kernel recovery rate (% of kernel weight to NIS weight) is the key quality metric. Industry target: 28–35% kernel recovery. Sound kernel recovery (SKR) must exceed 72% to meet export quality. Factors reducing quality: stink bug damage, premature nut drop, moisture stress during kernel development, and delayed processing after harvest. Nuts should be de-husked and dried to below 3.5% kernel moisture within 24 hours of harvest.

SAMAC (SA Macadamia Association): The industry body providing technical information, market intelligence, pest monitoring coordinaton, and annual cultivar evaluation data. All commercial macadamia growers are encouraged to register with SAMAC and access their Integrated Crop Protection Programme (ICPP).

Yields & returns: Mature orchards (12+ years): 3.5–6.0 t/ha NIS. At current prices (R60–R80/kg NIS), gross income exceeds R250,000–R400,000/ha for mature orchards. Production costs: R80,000–R120,000/ha/year for a mature orchard. Net profit potential makes macadamia one of SA's most profitable permanent crops — but only with professional management and patience through the establishment years.

SA pecan industry: South Africa has approximately 40,000 hectares of pecan orchards, with rapid expansion driven by strong export demand (primarily China and other Asian markets). Major growing areas include Limpopo (Hoedspruit, Groblersdal), North West (Vaalharts), and Northern Cape. Mpumalanga (warmer Lowveld) is suitable for pecans with irrigation.

Varieties: Wichita (dominant in SA — medium nut, thin shell, excellent flavour, early bearing, alternating bearer), Western Schley (premium thin-shell variety, excellent cracking quality), Choctaw (large nut, good cracking percentage), Navaho (consistent bearer, good quality), and Ukulinga (SA-bred, adapted to local conditions). Pecans are wind-pollinated but require cross-pollination from a compatible variety — Type I (protandrous) pollinates Type II (protogynous) and vice versa. Plant at least 2 compatible varieties in alternating rows. Wichita (Type I) is commonly paired with Western Schley or Choctaw (Type II).

Pollination requirements: Pecan relies entirely on wind pollination — no insect pollination needed. However, pollen viability is affected by temperature (optimal 24–30°C during pollination). Ensure pollinator trees are within 50–100m (pollen is heavy and doesn't travel far). A 10–15% pollinator tree ratio is recommended.

Water needs: Pecans are water-intensive trees. Mature trees require 1,000–1,400mm of water per year (rainfall + irrigation). In Mpumalanga's Lowveld, supplemental irrigation of 600–900mm/year is needed. Micro-sprinkler or drip irrigation. Water stress during nut fill (March–May) causes poorly filled kernels and reduced quality.

Establishment & spacing: Space 10m x 10m (100 trees/ha) or 12m x 6m (139 trees/ha) for higher-density systems with planned tree removal. Pecan trees are very large at maturity (15–20m canopy spread) — inadequate spacing leads to shading, reduced production, and disease. First commercial crop at year 6–8. Full production at year 12–15.

Nut quality: Kernel percentage (% kernel to in-shell nut weight) is critical — target above 50% for premium grades. Thin-shell varieties (Wichita, Western Schley) crack easily and achieve higher kernel percentages. Colour and flavour are affected by harvest timing (harvest promptly when shucks open and nuts fall) and drying (dry to 4–5% kernel moisture immediately after harvest).

Alternate bearing: Pecans are notorious for alternate bearing — heavy crop one year, light the next. Management strategies: adequate zinc nutrition (pecans are extremely zinc-hungry — apply ZnSO₄ as foliar spray at 3 kg/ha, 4–6 applications per season), mechanical fruit thinning in heavy-crop years, consistent irrigation, and balanced nitrogen (150–200 kg N/ha/year for mature trees).

Diseases & pests: Pecan scab (Venturia effusa) is the most serious disease — causes black spots on shucks and leaves, reducing nut size and quality. Fungicide programme (propiconazole, azoxystrobin) from budbreak through shell hardening. Aphids (black pecan aphid, yellow pecan aphid) can defoliate trees — monitor and spray when populations exceed thresholds.

Yields: Mature orchards: 2–4 t/ha in-shell (with proper management and irrigation). Top orchards exceed 5 t/ha. At current prices (R80–R120/kg in-shell), pecans are highly profitable at maturity.

Soil Health & Management Guide

Soil is the foundation of all crop production. Understanding, protecting, and building your soil's health is the single most impactful long-term investment a farmer can make. This section covers soil types in Mpumalanga, testing and interpretation, pH management, organic matter, nutrient management, and conservation tillage.

Soil types in Mpumalanga: The province's soils reflect its diverse geology and topography. The Highveld (Ermelo, Bethal, Standerton) features predominantly red and yellow-brown apedal soils (Hutton, Clovelly, Avalon forms in the SA soil classification) — well-drained, moderately fertile, suited to maize and grain production. The escarpment zone (Sabie, Graskop) has highly weathered, acidic soils with high organic matter. The Lowveld (Nelspruit, Komatipoort) ranges from deep red Hutton and Shortlands soils on dolerite (fertile, well-drained, excellent for citrus and subtropical fruit) to heavy vertic clays (Arcadia, Rensburg forms — high clay content, shrink-swell behaviour, challenging for drainage but fertile) and shallow lithic soils on granite.

Soil texture triangle: Soil texture describes the proportions of sand, silt, and clay. Sandy soils (>85% sand) drain quickly but hold little water or nutrients. Clay soils (>40% clay) hold water and nutrients but can waterlog, crust, and compact. Loam soils (balanced mix) are ideal for most crops. Knowing your soil texture helps guide irrigation scheduling, fertiliser management, and tillage practices.

Field texture assessment: Take a handful of moist soil. Sandy: gritty, won't form a ball. Sandy loam: forms a weak ball that crumbles easily. Loam: forms a firm ball, slightly sticky. Clay loam: forms a firm, sticky ball, can be rolled into a short ribbon. Clay: very sticky, plastic, forms a long thin ribbon (5cm+). This quick field test helps you characterise soils across your farm.

Soil depth assessment: Push a sharpened steel probe (soil auger or 12mm rebar rod) into the soil. Note depth of refusal or hard layer. Effective rooting depth should be at least 600mm for field crops, 900mm for fruit trees. Hard layers at 200–300mm may indicate plough pans from years of tillage at the same depth — break these with deep ripping (chisel plough to 400–500mm).

Drainage assessment: Dig a 500mm hole, fill with water, and time how long it takes to drain. Well-drained soil: drains within 1–4 hours. Moderately drained: 4–12 hours. Poorly drained: >12 hours. Grey or blue mottling in the soil profile indicates periodic waterlogging. Red and brown colours indicate well-aerated, well-drained soils.

How to take soil samples: Use a soil auger or spade to take cores from the 0–200mm depth (topsoil) and optionally 200–400mm (subsoil). Walk a zigzag pattern across each homogeneous land (similar soil, same crop history) and take 15–20 cores per sample. Mix cores in a clean bucket, remove stones and roots, and place 500g of the mixed sample in a labelled bag. Keep samples cool and deliver to the lab within 48 hours. Sample every 2–3 years, at the same time of year, ideally 3–6 months before planting so results are available for fertiliser planning.

What to test: A standard soil fertility analysis should include: pH (in KCl — the SA standard, approximately 0.5–1.0 unit lower than pH in water), phosphorus (P, Bray-1 method), potassium (K), calcium (Ca), magnesium (Mg), sodium (Na), organic carbon (%), and cation exchange capacity (CEC). Additional tests for problem soils: aluminium saturation, sulphur (S), zinc (Zn), boron (B), manganese (Mn), copper (Cu), iron (Fe), and molybdenum (Mo).

Interpreting results — key thresholds:

pH(KCl): Below 4.5 = severely acid (Al toxicity likely, urgent liming needed). 4.5–5.0 = acid (lime recommended for most crops). 5.0–5.5 = slightly acid (adequate for acid-tolerant crops like blueberries, tea). 5.5–6.5 = optimal range for most crops. Above 7.0 = alkaline (may need sulphur or acidifying fertilisers; micronutrient deficiencies possible).

Phosphorus (Bray-1, mg/kg): Below 8 = very low (dramatic crop response to P). 8–15 = low. 15–25 = medium (maintenance P applications). 25–35 = high. Above 35 = very high (no additional P needed; risk of environmental P runoff).

Potassium (mg/kg): Below 60 = deficient. 60–120 = low. 120–200 = adequate. Above 200 = high.

Organic carbon (%): Below 0.5% = very low (degraded soil). 0.5–1.5% = low (typical of heavily cultivated Highveld soils). 1.5–3.0% = moderate. Above 3.0% = high (virgin grassland or well-managed soil).

Accredited labs in SA: SGS (Bloemfontein), Bemlab (Strand), NviroTek (Hartbeespoort), Soil Health Solutions (Pietermaritzburg), and Cedara (KZN Department of Agriculture). Most labs return results within 7–14 working days. Cost: approximately R200–R500 per sample for a standard analysis.

Why pH matters: Soil pH controls the availability of virtually all plant nutrients. Most nutrients are maximally available between pH 5.5–6.5. Below pH 5.0, phosphorus, calcium, magnesium, and molybdenum become increasingly unavailable, while aluminium and manganese become toxic to roots. Above pH 7.5, iron, zinc, manganese, copper, and boron become less available.

Aluminium toxicity below pH 4.5: In strongly acid soils, soluble aluminium (Al³⁺) poisons root tips, preventing root elongation and branching. This restricts water and nutrient uptake, making the plant drought-susceptible even in wet soil. Acid saturation above 20% (the percentage of CEC occupied by H⁺ and Al³⁺) is damaging to most crops. At acid saturation above 40%, only the most tolerant crops survive.

Lime requirement calculation: The most accurate method is the acid saturation method: Lime (t/ha) = CEC × (current acid saturation − target acid saturation) × 0.16. Target acid saturation is typically 5–10% for most crops. The buffer pH method (using SMP or Shoemaker-McLean-Pratt buffer) is also used by some labs. Your lab report usually includes a lime recommendation — follow it.

Agricultural lime vs dolomitic lime: Calcitic/agricultural lime (CaCO₃) supplies calcium and neutralises acidity. Use when soil Ca is adequate or high but soil is acid. Dolomitic lime (CaCO₃·MgCO₃) supplies both calcium and magnesium — use when soil Mg is low (below 50 mg/kg or Ca:Mg ratio exceeds 8:1). Both neutralise soil acidity equally per tonne if of similar fineness.

Application timing & method: Apply lime 3–6 months before planting to allow time for the reaction to occur (lime reacts slowly, taking 2–6 months for full effect). Spread uniformly and incorporate into the top 150–200mm by discing or ploughing. Surface-applied lime on no-till fields corrects pH in the top 50mm first; deeper correction takes years. On established permanent crops (orchards, sugarcane), surface-apply and let rainfall move it into the root zone over time.

Fineness matters: Lime effectiveness depends on particle size — finer particles react faster. Specify lime with at least 70% passing a 0.25mm sieve. Coarse lime (pelletised or granular) is easier to spread but reacts more slowly. Apply at recommended rates — over-liming (pH above 7) can induce micronutrient deficiencies.

Building organic carbon: Soil organic matter (SOM, approximately 58% organic carbon) is the lifeblood of productive soil. It improves water-holding capacity (each 1% increase in organic carbon can hold an additional 20,000 litres of water per hectare), nutrient retention, soil structure, and biological activity. Most intensively farmed Highveld soils have organic carbon below 1.5% — well below the 3–5% of virgin grassland. Building organic carbon is slow (1–2% per decade with excellent management) but the most valuable long-term soil investment.

Composting: Convert kraal manure, crop residues, and other organic waste into stable compost. A well-managed compost windrow (C:N ratio 25–30:1, moisture 50–60%, turned weekly, temperatures reaching 55–65°C for pathogen kill) produces finished compost in 8–12 weeks. Apply 5–15 t/ha annually. Avoid raw manure on food crops — composting eliminates E. coli and weed seeds.

Cover crops: Plant cover crops during fallow periods to protect soil and add organic matter. Winter cover crops for the Mpumalanga Highveld: oats (Avena sativa), black oats, stooling rye (Secale cereale), grazing vetch (Vicia dasycarpa — a legume that fixes N and provides high-quality biomass). Summer cover crops: sun hemp (Crotalaria juncea), lab-lab (Lablab purpureus), Japanese radish/daikon (deep taproot breaks compaction). Multi-species cover crop cocktails (3–5 species combining grasses, legumes, and brassicas) maximise soil biological stimulation.

Crop residue management: Retain crop residues on the soil surface rather than burning or removing. A 5 t/ha maize residue blanket reduces soil temperature by 3–5°C, cuts evaporation by 30–50%, and provides food for soil organisms. Burning destroys organic matter, kills soil biology, and exposes soil to erosion — it should be avoided except where specific disease management requires it (e.g., sugarcane smut in some systems).

Mycorrhizal fungi: Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) form symbiotic relationships with 80–90% of crop plants, extending root networks and dramatically improving phosphorus and water uptake. Mycorrhizae are damaged by excessive tillage, high P fertilisation (above 50 mg/kg Bray-1), and long fallow periods. Reduce tillage, maintain living roots year-round (cover crops), and avoid excessive P to encourage mycorrhizal colonisation.

Earthworms as indicators: Earthworm populations are one of the best indicators of soil health. Healthy soil contains 25–50+ earthworms per spadeful (300mm x 300mm x 200mm). Low earthworm counts indicate acid soil, pesticide overuse, low organic matter, or compaction. Build earthworm populations by liming, adding organic matter, reducing tillage, and minimising broad-spectrum insecticide use.

Macronutrients:

Nitrogen (N): The most limiting nutrient in SA crop production. Essential for vegetative growth, leaf area, and protein synthesis. Sources: LAN (28% N), urea (46% N), ammonium sulphate (21% N + 24% S), compound fertilisers. N is mobile in soil — split applications reduce leaching and improve efficiency. Excess N causes lodging, delayed maturity, and environmental pollution.

Phosphorus (P): Critical for root development, energy transfer, and flowering. Immobile in soil — must be placed near roots. Sources: single superphosphate (SSP, 10% P + 12% S + 20% Ca), MAP (22% P + 11% N), DAP (20% P + 18% N), compound fertilisers. Buildup P to adequate levels (15–25 mg/kg Bray-1), then apply maintenance rates.

Potassium (K): Essential for water regulation, disease resistance, and grain quality. Moderately mobile. Sources: KCl/muriate of potash (50% K), potassium sulphate (42% K + 18% S), compound fertilisers. Most Mpumalanga Highveld soils have adequate K, but sandy soils and high-yielding crops can deplete K rapidly.

Secondary nutrients:

Calcium (Ca): Structural component of cell walls. Liming usually supplies adequate Ca. Deficiency signs: blossom-end rot in tomatoes, empty pods in groundnuts.

Magnesium (Mg): Central atom of chlorophyll. Deficiency: interveinal yellowing of older leaves (Mg is mobile — deficiency shows on old leaves first). Apply dolomitic lime or magnesium sulphate (Epsom salt) as foliar spray.

Sulphur (S): Essential for protein synthesis and oil quality in oilseeds. Deficiency resembles N deficiency but appears on younger leaves first (S is immobile). Sources: ammonium sulphate, gypsum, SSP, elemental sulphur.

Micronutrients:

Zinc (Zn): Most commonly deficient micronutrient in SA soils, especially on sandy, high-pH, or heavily limed soils. Deficiency: interveinal chlorosis, short internodes, small leaves. Apply 5–10 kg/ha ZnSO₄ to soil or 0.5% ZnSO₄ foliar spray. Critical for maize, wheat, and pecans.

Boron (B): Essential for cell division, flowering, and fruit/seed set. Deficiency: hollow stem in brassicas, poor kernel set in maize, cracked stems in celery. Apply 1–2 kg/ha as Solubor foliar spray. Narrow range between deficiency and toxicity — do not over-apply.

Manganese (Mn): Deficiency on high-pH or heavily limed soils — interveinal chlorosis similar to Fe deficiency. Foliar spray with MnSO₄ effective.

Copper (Cu), Iron (Fe), Molybdenum (Mo): Less commonly deficient in SA but important to test for if symptoms appear. Mo deficiency causes whiptail in cauliflower and poor N fixation in legumes (Mo is a component of the nitrogenase enzyme). Fe deficiency (lime-induced chlorosis) on calcareous soils — treat with chelated Fe.

Fertiliser types: Granular compound fertilisers (e.g., 2:3:4, 3:2:1, MAP) for base dressing. Single-element fertilisers (LAN, urea, KCl) for top-dressing and corrective applications. Liquid fertilisers for fertigation and foliar feeding. Organic fertilisers (compost, kraal manure, chicken litter) for building soil health — typically lower nutrient concentration but with added organic matter benefit.

No-till: Zero mechanical soil disturbance — seed is planted directly into untilled soil using specialised no-till planters. The soil surface remains covered with crop residues from the previous season. Benefits: reduces soil erosion by 80–95%, improves water infiltration by 50–100%, builds soil organic matter, reduces fuel and labour costs by 40–60%, and improves long-term soil structure. Challenges: requires investment in no-till planting equipment, skilled herbicide management for weed control, and a 3–5 year transition period during which yields may initially be similar or slightly lower as soil biology adjusts.

Minimum till: Reduces tillage intensity — typically one pass with a chisel plough or disc to 100–150mm (vs mouldboard ploughing to 200–250mm). Retains 30–60% of crop residues on the surface. A good intermediate step for farmers transitioning from conventional to no-till. Reduces erosion and fuel costs while maintaining some mechanical weed control.

Benefits proven in SA: Long-term trials by ARC and GrainSA on the Highveld show that after 5–10 years of continuous no-till: soil organic carbon increases by 0.3–0.8%, water-holding capacity improves by 15–25%, earthworm populations increase 3–5×, maize yields equal or exceed conventional tillage (with proper weed management), and input costs decrease by R1,000–R2,500/ha due to fuel and labour savings.

Equipment: No-till planters (e.g., Great Plains, John Deere, Equalizer) with coulter discs, seed openers, and heavy closing wheels designed to cut through residue and place seed accurately in untilled soil. For minimum-till: chisel ploughs, light discs. Avoid mouldboard ploughs (invert soil, bury organic matter, destroy soil structure).

Herbicide management: Without tillage for weed control, herbicides become the primary weed management tool. Pre-plant burndown with glyphosate or paraquat before planting. Pre-emergence residual herbicides (atrazine, metolachlor, S-metolachlor for maize; flumioxazin, metribuzin for soybeans). Post-emergence herbicides as needed. Rotate herbicide modes of action to prevent resistance development. Herbicide-resistant weeds are an increasing concern — integrate cover crops, crop rotation, and targeted hand-weeding to diversify weed control methods.

SA conservation agriculture programmes: GrainSA's Farmer Development Programme actively promotes conservation agriculture among developing farmers. The No-Till Club of South Africa facilitates knowledge sharing and farm visits. The ASSET (Agriculture Subsidised Sustainable Economic Transformation) and CA-FarmNet programmes provide training and mentoring. Contact GrainSA (www.grainsa.co.za) or your local agricultural extension officer for support in transitioning to conservation tillage.

Beef Cattle Production in South Africa

South Africa's beef cattle herd numbers approximately 14 million head, contributing over R50 billion annually to the agricultural economy. The country's diverse climatic zones — from the arid Karoo to the subtropical Lowveld and temperate Highveld — demand carefully matched breed selection. South Africa is uniquely positioned as a global leader in adapted beef genetics, with several world-renowned breeds developed here through decades of ARC research. Beef cattle farming ranges from extensive communal grazing systems supporting rural livelihoods to intensive commercial feedlot operations producing for both domestic and export markets.

Nguni

Origin & history: One of the oldest indigenous breeds in southern Africa, arriving with Bantu-speaking peoples over 2,000 years ago. Named after the Nguni-speaking peoples of KwaZulu-Natal. The ARC at Bartlow Combine has maintained a research herd since the 1930s, preserving diverse ecotypes.

Physical characteristics: Small to medium frame. Cows weigh 300–400 kg; bulls 500–600 kg. Distinctively multicoloured hides with over 100 recognised colour patterns, each carrying traditional Zulu names. Medium-length horns, cervico-thoracic hump, smooth short coat.

Advantages: Exceptional tick and tick-borne disease tolerance (heartwater, redwater, gallsickness) due to centuries of natural selection. Outstanding heat tolerance. Excellent foraging ability on poor-quality veld. Remarkable fertility — inter-calving period of 365–400 days even on natural veld. Low dystocia rates. Strong mothering instinct. Produces high-quality hides highly valued in the leather industry.

Limitations: Slower growth rates compared to exotic breeds (average daily gain 0.6–0.8 kg vs. 1.2+ kg for larger breeds). Lower weaning weights (150–180 kg). Smaller carcass.

Suitability: Ideal for extensive communal and small-scale farming systems where low-input management is essential. Excellent dam line for crossbreeding programmes. Thrives in bushveld, Lowveld, and subtropical coastal regions.

Afrikaner

Origin & history: Considered the oldest established breed in southern Africa, developed by Khoikhoi pastoralists and later refined by Afrikaner farmers from the 17th century. The Afrikaner Cattle Breeders' Society was established in 1912. The breed descends from the Bos taurus africanus group.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame, deep red colour with occasional darker shading. Cows 450–550 kg; bulls 700–900 kg. Lateral, lyre-shaped horns. Prominent cervico-thoracic hump. Loose, pliable skin with short, glossy coat.

Advantages: Exceptional hardiness and heat tolerance — thrives in temperatures exceeding 40°C. Highly resistant to ticks and tick-borne diseases. Excellent walking ability (crucial for extensive grazing). Strong hooves suited to rocky terrain. Outstanding fertility on natural veld. Excellent mothering ability with high calf survival rates. Long productive life (12–15 years).

Limitations: Somewhat slower growth rate than European breeds. Can be temperamental if not handled regularly. Horns can cause bruising in feedlot situations.

Suitability: Perfect for hot, dry extensive regions of the Northern Cape, Limpopo, and North West. Foundation dam line in the development of the Bonsmara. Well-suited to communal and commercial extensive systems.

Drakensberger

Origin & history: Indigenous breed with roots tracing to the black cattle kept by Khoikhoi and later developed by settlers in the Drakensberg mountain region. The breed society was established in 1947. A true dual-purpose heritage breed.

Physical characteristics: Medium to large frame. Solid black colour. Cows 450–550 kg; bulls 700–900 kg. Naturally horned (medium, forward-curved horns). Smooth coat, strong legs.

Advantages: Highly adaptable to different climatic regions across SA. Good tick resistance. Excellent walking ability. Dual-purpose potential — acceptable milk production for calf-rearing plus good beef conformation. Good temperament. Strong maternal traits with high weaning weights relative to cow size. Good feed conversion efficiency.

Limitations: Black colour can be a disadvantage in extremely hot, direct-sun environments. Smaller breed society and fewer available genetics compared to Bonsmara or Nguni.

Suitability: Well-suited to the mixed farming areas of KwaZulu-Natal, Eastern Cape, Free State, and Mpumalanga Highveld. Good for commercial farmers seeking an adapted, productive indigenous breed.

Tuli

Origin & history: Developed from indigenous Tswana cattle in the Tuli area of southern Zimbabwe (formerly Rhodesia) by the Amakhosi people. Introduced to South Africa from the 1940s. Selected for early maturity and meat quality.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame. Golden-yellow to red colour. Cows 400–500 kg; bulls 600–800 kg. Polled or short-horned. Smooth coat, loose skin, well-developed dewlap.

Advantages: Early sexual maturity — heifers can breed at 14–15 months. Excellent meat tenderness (consistently ranks highly in taste panels). Good heat tolerance. Efficient feed converters. Docile temperament. Polled variants reduce bruising risk. Excellent crossbreeding results with European breeds.

Limitations: Smaller frame limits finished carcass weight. Less widely available in SA than Nguni or Bonsmara. Susceptibility to some tick-borne diseases higher than Nguni.

Suitability: Good for bushveld and subtropical regions. Valued as a dam line for producing early-maturing, tender-meated slaughter animals in crossbreeding programmes.

Bonsmara

Origin & history: South Africa's most iconic developed breed, created by Prof Jan Bonsma at the ARC Mara Research Station in Limpopo from the 1930s–1960s. The breed composition is 5/8 Afrikaner, 3/16 Hereford, and 3/16 Shorthorn. Bonsma's pioneering work on functional efficiency and adaptability testing laid the scientific foundation for tropical beef cattle breeding worldwide. The Bonsmara Breeders' Society was established in 1964.

Physical characteristics: Medium to large frame. Red to cherry-red colour with occasional darker shading. Cows 500–600 kg; bulls 800–1,000 kg. Naturally polled or short-horned. Well-muscled, balanced conformation with good length and depth. Smooth coat, pliable skin.

Advantages: Combines the hardiness and adaptability of the Afrikaner with the growth and meat quality of British breeds. Exceptional adaptability to South African conditions — performs from the Kalahari to the Lowveld. Good tick tolerance. Excellent fertility (ICP 380–400 days). High weaning weights (200–240 kg). Excellent feedlot performance (ADG 1.4–1.8 kg). Good temperament. Largest beef breed society in SA with over 80,000 animals registered annually.

Limitations: Requires reasonable nutrition to express its genetic potential. Not as heat/tick tolerant as purebred Nguni or Afrikaner. Premium genetics come at higher purchase prices.

Suitability: The most versatile breed for South African commercial beef production. Suitable for extensive, semi-intensive, and feedlot systems across most regions. Excellent for both purebred and crossbreeding programmes.

Beefmaster SA

Origin & history: Originally developed in Texas, USA, by Tom Lasater from Brahman, Hereford, and Shorthorn genetics. Established in South Africa since the 1970s. Selected on the "Six Essentials": fertility, disposition, weight, conformation, hardiness, and milk production.

Physical characteristics: Large frame. Variable colour — red, dun, or red-and-white. Cows 550–650 kg; bulls 900–1,100 kg. Polled or horned. Moderate hump, loose skin.

Advantages: Outstanding fertility — cows are selected to breed back within 65 days of calving. Good milk production for calf growth. Excellent growth rates (ADG 1.5–1.8 kg in feedlot). Good heat tolerance due to Brahman influence. Docile disposition (selection criterion). Good hybrid vigour in crossbreeding.

Limitations: Less adapted than SA indigenous breeds. Variable phenotype due to multi-breed origin. Larger frame requires higher nutritional plane.

Suitability: Well-suited to semi-intensive and intensive systems in bushveld and subtropical areas. Good dam line for commercial herds focusing on weaning weight.

Simbra

Origin & history: A composite breed combining Simmentaler and Brahman genetics, developed in South Africa from the 1960s. Typically 5/8 Simmentaler x 3/8 Brahman, though ratios vary. Recognised as a breed with its own society since 2005.

Physical characteristics: Large frame. Variable colour — red, fawn, or red-and-white, often with darker head/ears. Cows 550–650 kg; bulls 900–1,100 kg. Moderate Brahman-type hump and ear length. Well-muscled.

Advantages: Hybrid vigour combining Simmentaler growth and muscling with Brahman heat/tick tolerance. Excellent growth rates. Good milk production. Adaptable to subtropical conditions. Performs well in feedlot (ADG 1.6–2.0 kg). Good carcass quality with high meat yield.

Limitations: Variable phenotype in early generations. Brahman influence can bring temperament challenges. Requires stabilisation across generations.

Suitability: Ideal for subtropical and bushveld regions where both productivity and heat adaptation are needed. Popular in Limpopo, Mpumalanga Lowveld, and KwaZulu-Natal.

Santa Gertrudis SA

Origin & history: Developed on the King Ranch in Texas, USA, from Shorthorn and Brahman. Introduced to SA in the 1950s. The breed has been further adapted to local conditions over seven decades.

Physical characteristics: Large frame. Cherry-red colour. Cows 550–650 kg; bulls 850–1,000 kg. Short, smooth coat. Moderate loose skin. Short horns or polled.

Advantages: Good heat tolerance. Fast growth rates. Efficient feed conversion. Good carcass quality with even fat cover. Docile temperament. Good walking ability. Well-adapted to SA bushveld conditions after decades of local selection.

Limitations: Less tick-tolerant than Brahman or indigenous breeds. Can be sensitive to cold in Highveld winter conditions. Smaller gene pool in SA compared to Bonsmara.

Suitability: Semi-intensive and intensive systems in warmer regions. Good terminal sire in crossbreeding programmes.

Simmentaler

Origin: Switzerland (Simme Valley). One of Europe's oldest and most widely distributed breeds. Introduced to SA in the 1900s, now one of the most popular breeds in the country.

Physical characteristics: Large frame — the largest of the European breeds commonly used in SA. Red-and-white or golden-red. Cows 600–750 kg; bulls 1,000–1,300 kg. Horned or polled.

Advantages: Exceptional growth rate (ADG 1.6–2.0 kg). True dual-purpose breed with excellent milk production (cows can produce 3,000–4,000 kg milk per lactation). Outstanding muscling and carcass yield (dressing percentage 58–62%). Extremely popular as a crossbreeding sire over indigenous and Brahman-type cows to boost growth and milk. Good temperament.

Limitations: Poor heat tolerance — suffers in temperatures above 32°C. Susceptible to ticks and tick-borne diseases. Light skin susceptible to sunburn and skin cancer. High nutritional requirements. Large frame increases dystocia risk when mated to small-framed cows.

Suitability: Best suited to temperate Highveld, irrigated pastures, and intensive systems. Excellent terminal sire. Not recommended for extensive hot/subtropical systems as a purebred.

Hereford

Origin: Herefordshire, England. One of the world's most numerous breeds. Established in SA since the early 1900s.

Physical characteristics: Medium to large frame. Dark red body with white face, chest, belly, and lower legs. Cows 500–600 kg; bulls 800–1,000 kg. Horned or polled.

Advantages: Excellent feedlot efficiency and feed conversion. Docile temperament — among the calmest of all beef breeds. Good marbling and meat quality. Early maturity. The white face is a dominant genetic trait, useful for identifying crossbred calves.

Limitations: Highly susceptible to sunburn, skin cancer (particularly around eyes and udder), and photosensitivity — a serious limitation in SA's intense UV environment. Poor tick tolerance. Susceptible to tick-borne diseases. Not suited to hot, extensive systems.

Suitability: Feedlots and temperate Highveld regions with moderate temperatures. Good terminal sire over adapted cows.

Angus

Origin: Scotland (Aberdeen and Angus). Introduced to SA from the early 1900s. Growing rapidly in popularity due to branded beef programmes (e.g., Certified Angus Beef).

Physical characteristics: Medium frame. Solid black (or red for Red Angus). Cows 450–550 kg; bulls 750–950 kg. Naturally polled (hornless) — a major practical advantage.

Advantages: Outstanding meat quality with superior marbling — consistently the top-ranked breed for eating quality. Early maturity. Calving ease (moderate birth weights of 30–35 kg). Polled reduces bruising and handling injuries. Excellent feedlot performance. Black hide provides better UV protection than Hereford. Strong branded beef marketing programmes create premium market access.

Limitations: Black colour absorbs heat — can suffer in extremely hot environments. Moderate tick tolerance only. Lower growth rates than Simmentaler or Charolais. Smaller carcass than continental breeds.

Suitability: Feedlots, temperate regions, and premium beef programmes. Excellent terminal sire for marbling. Red Angus variant better suited to warmer SA conditions.

Charolais

Origin: Central France (Charolles region). Introduced to SA in the 1950s.

Physical characteristics: Very large frame — among the largest beef breeds. White to cream colour. Cows 600–750 kg; bulls 1,000–1,300 kg. Heavily muscled, particularly hindquarters. Horned or polled.

Advantages: Exceptional muscling and lean meat yield. Highest growth rates of all European breeds (ADG 1.8–2.2 kg). Excellent dressing percentage (60–63%). Lean carcass with minimal waste fat. Outstanding terminal sire for maximising carcass weight.

Limitations: Dystocia risk — heavy birth weights (38–45 kg) can cause calving difficulty, especially on first-calf heifers or small-framed cows. Light skin highly susceptible to sunburn and cancer. Very poor heat/tick tolerance. Requires high nutritional plane. Can be temperamental.

Suitability: Strictly a terminal sire breed in SA — used over mature, adapted cows for slaughter-generation calves. Feedlots and intensive finishing systems.

Limousin

Origin: Limousin region, France. Established in SA since the 1970s.

Physical characteristics: Medium to large frame. Golden-red to wheat colour. Cows 550–650 kg; bulls 900–1,100 kg. Well-muscled, fine-boned. Horned or polled.

Advantages: Exceptionally lean meat — highest meat-to-bone ratio of any breed. Fine bone structure means more saleable meat per kg carcass. Good calving ease relative to carcass weight. Good feed conversion. Lighter pigmented skin than Charolais gives better sun tolerance.

Limitations: Can be temperamental (nervous disposition). Moderate heat tolerance. Not suited to extensive hot systems. Smaller gene pool in SA.

Suitability: Terminal sire for lean meat production. Feedlots and semi-intensive temperate systems.

Sussex

Origin: Sussex, England. Established in SA since the 1800s — one of the earliest imported breeds.

Physical characteristics: Medium to large frame. Rich, dark red colour. Cows 550–650 kg; bulls 850–1,000 kg. Naturally polled in modern strains.

Advantages: Among the best-adapted European breeds for SA conditions. Good heat tolerance for a British breed (dark skin and pigment). Excellent temperament. Good foraging ability. Moderate nutritional requirements compared to continental breeds. Good mothering and milk production. Versatile for both extensive and intensive systems.

Limitations: Growth rate slightly below Simmentaler/Charolais. Less fashionable — smaller gene pool and fewer bulls available.

Suitability: One of the most practical European breeds for SA conditions. Good for Eastern Cape, KZN Midlands, and cooler Highveld. Versatile in both purebred and crossbreeding systems.

Brahman

Origin: Developed in the USA from Indian Bos indicus cattle (Gir, Guzerat, Nellore, Krishna Valley). Introduced to SA in the 1950s. The SA Brahman Breeders' Society is one of the largest breed societies in the country.

Physical characteristics: Large frame. Colour ranges from light grey to dark grey, red, or near-black. Cows 500–600 kg; bulls 800–1,100 kg. Prominent hump over the shoulders. Large, pendulous ears. Loose, pliable skin with abundant dewlap.

Advantages: Superior heat tolerance — the benchmark breed for tropical/subtropical environments. Exceptional tick resistance due to skin secretions, coat type, and grooming behaviour. Resistant to most tick-borne diseases. Remarkable walking ability. Efficient foragers on poor-quality veld. Long productive life. Unmatched as a crossbreeding sire for introducing heat/tick adaptation — crosses with European breeds produce outstanding F1 hybrid vigour (15–25% improvement in weaning weight).

Limitations: Can be nervous and difficult to handle if not habituated to human contact from a young age. Longer gestation (290–295 days vs. 283 for Bos taurus). Later sexual maturity (heifers 24–30 months). Meat tenderness can be lower due to higher calpastatin levels. Some carcass traits (larger hump, more internal fat) reduce saleable meat yield.

Suitability: Essential breed for the subtropical Lowveld, bushveld, and Limpopo regions. Primarily used in crossbreeding programmes — pure Brahman commercial herds are less common. Foundation breed for the Simbra, Brangus, and other composites.

Boran

Origin: Developed by the Borana people of southern Ethiopia and northern Kenya over centuries of natural selection. Introduced to SA in the early 2000s. Growing rapidly in popularity.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame. Usually white, fawn, or light grey, occasionally red. Cows 400–500 kg; bulls 650–850 kg. Small, neat head. Short horns. Small cervico-thoracic hump. Tight skin with short, sleek coat.

Advantages: Outstanding tick resistance — research from Kenya shows Boran carry fewer ticks than most Bos indicus breeds. Exceptional disease resistance (East Coast fever, trypanosomiasis in East Africa). Notably docile temperament — a significant advantage over Brahman. Good fertility and mothering ability. Efficient feed converters relative to body size. Moderate maintenance requirements. Good walking ability.

Limitations: Smaller frame and lower growth rates than Brahman. Less widely available in SA — still a relatively new breed. Fewer performance data and EBVs available from SA Stud Book compared to established breeds.

Suitability: Excellent for bushveld and subtropical regions where tick resistance and docility are priorities. Growing role as an alternative to Brahman in crossbreeding programmes, particularly where temperament is a concern.

Stocking Rates by Region

Sweetveld (Lowveld, bushveld): 4–10 ha per Large Stock Unit (LSU), depending on rainfall and bush density. Sweetveld is palatable year-round but vulnerable to overgrazing. Stocking rates above the recommended level cause irreversible bush encroachment.

Sourveld (Highveld grasslands): 2–5 ha/LSU. Sourveld becomes unpalatable and low in nutrition after frost (May–September), making winter supplementation compulsory. The grass is highly nutritious during summer growth (November–March).

Mixed veld: 3–6 ha/LSU. Contains both sweet and sour components. Grazing management should prioritise the sour component during summer and sweet areas during winter.

Karoo & arid areas: 10–30 ha/LSU. Extremely sensitive to overgrazing. Stock conservatively and destock early during drought.

Supplementation

Winter protein lick (sourveld): Essential from May to September. Provide a lick containing 30–40% crude protein (from urea and natural protein sources), salt, and minerals. Target intake: 250–400 g/cow/day. This stimulates rumen microbial activity to digest low-quality standing hay.

Summer phosphorus lick: Critical across most of SA — phosphorus deficiency is the most widespread mineral deficiency in South African cattle. Provide a salt-phosphate lick (8–12% P) year-round in deficient areas. Deficiency causes pica (bone chewing), reduced fertility, and poor growth.

Production lick: For growing heifers, pregnant cows in late gestation, or lactating cows on poor veld. Contains energy (maize meal), protein (oilcake), and minerals. Target 1–2 kg/animal/day.

Drought feeding: During drought, provide hay (eragrostis or grass hay at 8–10 kg/day) plus a protein/energy supplement. Destock early — it is always cheaper to sell than feed. DAFF disaster management assistance may be available during declared droughts.

Rotational Grazing & Camp Systems

Multi-camp system: Divide grazing into at least 4–8 camps per herd. Graze each camp for 7–14 days, then rest for 30–60 days. This allows grass to recover its leaf area and root reserves. ARC research shows rotational grazing increases veld productivity by 20–40% compared to continuous grazing.

High-density, short-duration grazing: Some farmers achieve excellent results with 30+ camps, grazing at very high density for 1–3 days with long rest periods (60–120 days). Requires good water infrastructure in every camp.

Water requirements: Cattle need 40–70 litres/day depending on temperature, lactation status, and feed moisture. Provide clean water within 3 km of any grazing point. A water source per camp is ideal. Contaminated water reduces intake and spreads disease.

Vaccination Schedule (DAFF/ARC Recommended)

Brucellosis (CA strain): Compulsory vaccination of all heifer calves between 4 and 8 months of age. Controlled disease under the Animal Diseases Act. Only registered veterinarians or authorised persons may vaccinate. Vaccinated animals must be branded with a "B" on the left jaw and recorded.

Anthrax: Annual vaccination essential in endemic areas (Limpopo, North West, KZN, northern Free State, Mpumalanga). Vaccinate all cattle over 6 months annually. Use Sterne strain vaccine. Anthrax is a zoonotic disease (transmissible to humans) — report suspected cases immediately to state veterinarian.

Blackquarter (Black leg): Vaccinate calves at 3 months, booster at 6 months, then annually. Common in young cattle (6–24 months) grazing on disturbed soil. Caused by Clostridium chauvoei.

Botulism: Annual vaccination with bivalent C&D toxoid. Critical in phosphorus-deficient areas where cattle chew bones (pica). Botulism kills rapidly — often found dead with no prior symptoms.

Lumpy skin disease: Vaccinate annually before summer (September–October). Spread by biting insects. Causes production losses, hide damage, and occasionally death. Live attenuated Neethling vaccine.

Rift Valley fever: Vaccinate during outbreaks as directed by state veterinary services. Outbreaks occur in wet years following heavy rains that breed mosquito vectors. Zoonotic — handle infected animals with care.

Tick-Borne Diseases

Heartwater (Ehrlichia ruminantium): Transmitted by the bont tick (Amblyomma hebraeum). Prevalent in bushveld and Lowveld. Symptoms: high fever, nervous signs, staggering, recumbency. Mortality 50–90% in susceptible animals. Treat with oxytetracycline if caught early. Block vaccine (blood from infected sheep) available but risky. Indigenous breeds show significant natural resistance.

Redwater (Babesia bigemina/bovis): Transmitted by blue ticks (Boophilus/Rhipicephalus). Causes red urine, fever, anaemia, and rapid death. Treat with diminazene aceturate (Berenil) or imidocarb. Endemic stability — calves under 9 months rarely show clinical disease and develop lasting immunity.

Gallsickness (Anaplasma marginale): Transmitted by various ticks and biting flies. Causes anaemia, jaundice, fever. Chronic carrier state is common. Treat with oxytetracycline. Older animals (>2 years) exposed for the first time are most at risk.

Tick control strategy: Aim for endemic stability rather than tick eradication — allow low-level tick exposure to maintain natural immunity, particularly in calves. Dip or treat strategically (every 7–14 days in heavy seasons) rather than intensively. Rotate acaricide chemical groups to prevent resistance. Pour-on, spray-race, and plunge dip methods each have advantages depending on herd size.

Internal Parasites & FMD

Internal parasites: Deworm strategically — typically at the start of the rainy season (October) and at the end of the wet season (April/May). Use faecal egg counts (FEC) to guide treatment rather than blanket dosing. Rotate between anthelmintic drug classes (white, clear, yellow groups). Liver fluke (Fasciola) is important in wet, marshy areas — treat with specific flukicides.

Foot-and-mouth disease (FMD): South Africa has FMD-free zones (most of the country) and FMD control/protection zones (areas bordering Kruger National Park in Mpumalanga and Limpopo). In control zones, cattle must be vaccinated biannually, movement is strictly controlled, and veterinary inspections are compulsory. Never move animals across FMD zone boundaries without a veterinary permit.

Breeding Management

Breeding season: Limit to 60–90 days (e.g., December–February in summer rainfall areas). This produces a concentrated calving season (September–November) which simplifies management, allows uniform calf crops, enables group marketing at weaning, and ensures calves are born when veld nutrition peaks.

Bull:cow ratio: 1:25 to 1:30 for mature bulls in multi-sire extensive systems. 1:30 to 1:40 for single-sire systems with smaller camps. Young bulls (2–3 years): 1:15 maximum. Always conduct a breeding soundness examination (BSE) on all bulls 6–8 weeks before the mating season. A BSE includes scrotal measurement (minimum 32 cm at 2 years), semen evaluation, physical examination, and serving capacity test.

Artificial insemination (AI): Allows access to superior genetics at a fraction of the cost of purchasing elite bulls. Requires good infrastructure (crush, races, facilities), skilled technicians, and reliable heat detection. Fixed-time AI (FTAI) protocols using hormone synchronisation eliminate the need for heat detection and achieve 45–55% conception rates per insemination. AI is particularly valuable for introducing specific traits (e.g., marbling from Angus, milk from Simmentaler).

Pregnancy testing: Rectally palpate or ultrasound all cows 6–8 weeks after the end of the breeding season. Cull open (non-pregnant) cows — they consume feed without producing a calf. Target: 90%+ pregnancy rate in well-managed herds.

Calving & Weaning

Calving management: Move cows to clean calving camps 2–4 weeks before expected calving. Check heifers at least twice daily. Intervene only when necessary — most indigenous and adapted breeds calve unassisted. Ensure calf receives colostrum within 6 hours of birth (critical for passive immunity). Dip navels in 7% iodine solution.

Weaning: Wean calves at 7–8 months (200–210 days). Target weaning weight depends on breed: Nguni 150–180 kg, Bonsmara 200–240 kg, Simmentaler crosses 220–260 kg. Abrupt weaning is simplest — separate calves completely. Provide good-quality hay and a starter lick/creep feed during the transition. Vaccinate and deworm at weaning.

Record Keeping & Performance Testing

SA Stud Book: The national animal recording and improvement organisation. Maintains pedigree records, performance data, and calculates Estimated Breeding Values (EBVs) for registered animals. Participation in Logix performance recording is essential for stud breeders and highly recommended for commercial producers.

Essential records: Identify every animal (ear tags, brands, or tattoos). Record: date of birth, sire and dam, birth weight, weaning weight (205-day adjusted), yearling weight, calving date, calving ease score, all vaccinations/treatments, and disposal (sale, death, cause). These records enable selection decisions based on data rather than appearance.

Key performance indicators: Inter-calving period (target <400 days), weaning percentage (calves weaned ÷ cows mated × 100; target >85%), kg calf weaned per LSU per hectare (the ultimate measure of extensive beef system efficiency), and average daily gain in feedlot.

Dairy Cattle Production in South Africa

South Africa's dairy industry produces approximately 3.3 billion litres of milk annually from around 1,600 commercial dairy herds. The industry is concentrated in the Western Cape, Eastern Cape, KwaZulu-Natal, and cooler Highveld regions of the Free State and Mpumalanga. Dairy farming is capital and management intensive, demanding consistent daily attention to nutrition, milking hygiene, and herd health. The Milk Producers' Organisation (MPO) supports the industry, while Milk SA manages statutory levies and research.

Holstein / Friesian

Characteristics: Large frame (cows 600–700 kg). Black-and-white or red-and-white. The world's highest-producing dairy breed. SA Holsteins average 8,000–10,000 kg/lactation in well-managed herds; top herds exceed 12,000 kg/lactation. Butterfat 3.4–3.6%, protein 3.1–3.3%.

Advantages: Unmatched milk volume. Well-established genetics with extensive genomic data. Wide availability of semen (local and imported). Strong breed improvement infrastructure through SA Holstein Breeders' Society.

Limitations: Heat sensitive — production drops significantly above 25°C (heat stress begins at THI >72). Requires high-quality nutrition (TMR or high-concentrate feeding). Susceptible to metabolic disorders (ketosis, milk fever, displaced abomasum). Fertility declines at very high production levels. Unsuitable for extensive or low-input systems.

Jersey

Characteristics: Small frame (cows 380–450 kg). Fawn to dark brown with black muzzle. SA Jerseys produce 5,500–7,000 kg/lactation. Butterfat 4.8–5.5%, protein 3.6–3.9% — the highest component percentages of any commercial breed.

Advantages: Better heat tolerance than Holstein — the preferred breed in warmer SA dairying regions (KZN, Mpumalanga). Produces more milk solids per kg body weight than any breed. Lower feed costs per litre of component-corrected milk. Better fertility and longevity than Holstein. Calving ease (small calves). Highly profitable in payment systems based on milk solids rather than volume.

Limitations: Lower total volume — disadvantageous in volume-based payment systems. Small calves have low beef value. Susceptible to milk fever due to high calcium output. Can be nervous in temperament.

Ayrshire

Characteristics: Medium frame (cows 500–550 kg). Red-and-white with distinctive patterns. Production 6,000–8,000 kg/lactation. Butterfat 3.9–4.1%, protein 3.3–3.5%.

Advantages: Robust and hardy with strong feet and legs. Good longevity — often stays in the herd longer than Holstein. Moderate feed requirements. Good grazing ability for pasture-based systems. Resistant to udder breakdown. Produces well-balanced milk for cheese making.

Limitations: Lower peak production than Holstein. Can be strong-willed in temperament. Smaller gene pool in SA.

Other Breeds & Crossbreeds

Guernsey: Medium frame, golden-fawn colour. Produces golden-coloured milk with high beta-carotene. Butterfat 4.5–5.0%. Small SA population but valued for niche markets.

Brown Swiss: Large frame, grey-brown colour. Excellent longevity and feet/legs. Good milk protein for cheese. Growing interest in SA for crossbreeding with Holstein to improve robustness.

Crossbreeding: Holstein × Jersey (Kiwicross type) gaining popularity, combining Holstein volume with Jersey components, fertility, and heat tolerance. Three-way crosses using Scandinavian breeds (Swedish Red, Norwegian Red) for improved health traits are also being explored by progressive SA dairy farmers.

Milking routine: Milk twice daily at consistent 12-hour intervals (e.g., 04:00 and 16:00). Some high-producing herds milk three times daily for a 10–15% production increase. Consistent routine reduces stress and maximises letdown.

Parlour hygiene: Pre-dip teats with approved teat disinfectant. Strip first 2–3 squirts to check for clinical mastitis (clots, discolouration). Attach cups within 60–90 seconds of stimulation. Post-dip all teats immediately after cup removal. Clean and sanitise all equipment after every milking.

Mastitis prevention: Mastitis is the most costly disease in dairy farming. Monitor somatic cell count (SCC) — bulk tank target below 300,000 cells/ml; individual cow target below 200,000. Conduct California Mastitis Test (CMT) monthly. Dry-cow therapy (antibiotic infusion at dry-off) is standard practice. Cull chronic high-SCC cows. Maintain clean, dry housing — wet, muddy conditions cause environmental mastitis (E. coli, Strep. uberis).

Milk cooling: Cool raw milk to below 4°C within 2 hours of milking. Bulk tanks must maintain 4°C or below. Cooling chain integrity is essential for milk quality grading. Plate coolers pre-cool milk before it enters the tank, saving energy.

Total mixed ration (TMR): The standard feeding system for high-producing herds. Balance energy (maize silage, hominy chop, maize grain), protein (soybean oilcake, sunflower oilcake, canola meal), roughage (eragrostis hay, lucerne, oat hay), and minerals/vitamins. A nutritionist should formulate rations — imbalances cause metabolic disease and lost production. Target 55–60% roughage: 40–45% concentrate by dry matter for high producers.

Pasture-based systems: Kikuyu, ryegrass, fescue, or clover-based pastures with strategic concentrate supplementation (4–8 kg/cow/day). Rotational grazing in small paddocks (12–24 hour shifts) maximises pasture utilisation. Well-managed pastures can support 4–6 cows/ha with supplementation. Pasture-based systems have lower production per cow but lower input costs.

Transition cow feeding: The 3-week period before and after calving is the highest-risk period. Feed a transition/close-up ration with controlled calcium (to prevent milk fever), anionic salts, and increased energy density. After calving, increase concentrate gradually (0.5 kg/day increments) to prevent acidosis. Peak energy demand occurs 6–8 weeks into lactation.

Dry cow management: Dry period of 50–60 days is essential for udder tissue regeneration and foetal growth. Two phases: far-off dry (lower energy, high fibre) and close-up (increasing energy for calving). Provide adequate trace minerals (selenium, zinc, copper, vitamin E) for immune function.

Water: Lactating cows require 80–150 litres/day (more in hot weather). Provide unlimited access to clean, fresh water. Water quality affects intake — test for salinity, nitrates, and bacterial contamination. Position water troughs so cows can drink immediately after milking.

AI and heat detection: Over 80% of SA dairy herds use artificial insemination. Accurate heat detection is the single most important factor for reproductive success. Signs: standing to be mounted, clear mucus discharge, restlessness, chin-resting. Activity monitors (pedometers, neck collars) detect 85–95% of heats. Inseminate 8–12 hours after first standing heat. Conception rate target: 45–55% per insemination.

Calving interval target: 365–380 days. This requires conception within 85–100 days after calving. Voluntary waiting period of 50–60 days post-calving allows uterine recovery. Cows not pregnant by 150 days in milk should be evaluated for culling or hormonal intervention.

Calf rearing: Separate calf from dam within 12–24 hours. Ensure 4 litres of high-quality colostrum within 6 hours of birth (check quality with Brix refractometer — target ≥22%). Feed whole milk or quality milk replacer at 10–15% of body weight daily. Introduce calf starter concentrate from day 3. Wean when consuming 1.5–2 kg starter/day (typically 56–70 days). Target: double birth weight by weaning. Dehorn and remove extra teats at 4–8 weeks.

Heifer management: Target first calving at 24 months — this requires heifers to reach breeding weight (60% of mature weight) by 13–15 months. Holstein heifers should weigh 370–400 kg at breeding; Jersey heifers 250–270 kg. Monitor growth with regular weighing or heart-girth tape measurements. Under-grown heifers delay first calving and lifetime productivity.

Goat Farming in South Africa

South Africa has approximately 6 million goats, with the majority kept in communal farming systems in the Eastern Cape, Limpopo, KwaZulu-Natal, and North West. South Africa is also the world's largest producer of mohair (from Angora goats) and developed the Boer goat — the world's premier meat goat breed. Goats are browsers rather than grazers, efficiently converting bush and browse into high-quality protein. They are well-suited to communal and small-scale farming, and are increasingly farmed commercially for meat, milk, and fibre.

Boer Goat

Origin & history: Developed in South Africa's Eastern Cape from indigenous and imported goat genetics over a century of selective breeding. The SA Boer Goat Breeders' Society was established in 1959. Recognised worldwide as the premier meat goat breed — exported to over 70 countries.

Physical characteristics: Large frame. White body with distinctive red/brown head and neck (some full red or paint-type variants). Mature does 60–80 kg; bucks 100–130 kg. Roman nose, pendulous ears, strong legs. Well-muscled hindquarters and loin.

Advantages: Fastest growth rate of any goat breed — kids reach 30–35 kg at 100 days. Excellent carcass conformation with 48–52% dressing percentage. High twinning rate (60–80% of kiddings). Good mothering ability and milk production for kid rearing. Docile temperament. Adaptable to various climatic conditions. Good grazing and browsing ability.

Limitations: Susceptible to internal parasites (Haemonchus) due to high nutritional demands. White body can suffer sunburn in intense environments. Requires reasonable management and nutrition to express genetic potential. Premium stud animals are expensive (R10,000–R100,000+ for top genetics).

Suitability: All regions of SA. Excellent for commercial meat production. Ideal for small-scale and emerging farmers seeking quick returns. Outstanding crossbreeding sire over indigenous does.

Savanna

Origin: Developed in the Northern Cape and Limpopo bushveld from indigenous white goats. Breed society established in 1993.

Physical characteristics: Large frame, all white with fully pigmented (black) skin, hooves, and horns. Does 60–75 kg; bucks 90–120 kg. Short, sleek coat. Strong legs and hard hooves.

Advantages: Fully pigmented skin provides excellent UV/sun protection — superior to Boer goat in this regard. Hardier than Boer goat with lower parasite burden. Excellent mothering and milk production. Good twinning rate. White colour preferred by some cultural markets. Outstanding browsing ability and walking endurance. Lower maintenance requirements than Boer goat.

Limitations: Slightly slower growth rate than Boer goat. Less widely available internationally. Fewer performance data than Boer goat.

Suitability: Hot, dry, and extensive bushveld regions. Excellent for farmers wanting a low-maintenance, hardy meat goat. Good alternative to Boer goat in sun-intense environments.

Kalahari Red

Origin: Developed from red-headed indigenous goats in the Kalahari region. Breed society established in 1999.

Physical characteristics: Large frame, uniformly red/reddish-brown. Does 55–75 kg; bucks 85–115 kg. Short coat, pigmented skin. Strong, hard hooves.

Advantages: Red colour provides natural camouflage against predators (especially jackals and eagles) — a significant advantage in extensive systems. Fully pigmented skin — excellent sun tolerance. Very hardy with low parasite susceptibility. Good browsing ability. Adapted to arid conditions. Good mothering instinct. Kids are well-camouflaged at birth, reducing predation losses.

Limitations: Smaller than Boer goat, lower growth rate. Colour can be inconsistent in early breeding lines. Smaller gene pool.

Suitability: Arid and semi-arid regions (Kalahari, Karoo, Northern Cape, Limpopo). Ideal where predation is a major concern and low-input management is required.

Indigenous Veld Goat

Origin: Collective term for the unimproved indigenous goats of southern Africa, including regional ecotypes such as the Eastern Cape Xhosa goat, the Zulu goat, and the Northern Cape goat. The Indigenous Veld Goat Society preserves these genetics.

Physical characteristics: Small to medium frame. Variable colours and patterns. Does 30–45 kg; bucks 45–65 kg. Hardy conformation with strong legs. Adapted to local conditions over centuries.

Advantages: Maximum hardiness and disease resistance. Lowest input requirements. Outstanding tick and parasite tolerance. Excellent foragers on poor-quality browse. Strong cultural and traditional value — essential for ceremonies, lobola, and community events. High fertility in harsh conditions. Can survive where improved breeds cannot.

Limitations: Small frame, slow growth rate, small carcass. Low commercial value per animal. Limited formal performance recording.

Suitability: Communal farming systems, food-security projects, and as a genetic reservoir for crossbreeding with Boer goat bucks to combine hardiness with growth.

Saanen

Origin: Saanen Valley, Switzerland. The world's highest-producing dairy goat breed. Established in SA since the early 1900s.

Physical characteristics: Large frame. Pure white or cream. Does 60–80 kg; bucks 80–100 kg. Short, fine coat. Erect ears. Hornless (polled) in most strains.

Advantages: Highest milk yield — 800–1,200 litres per 300-day lactation in well-managed SA herds. Top producers exceed 1,500 litres. Butterfat 3.0–3.5%. Docile temperament. Good for parlour milking systems. Widely available genetics.

Limitations: Unpigmented (white) skin — highly susceptible to sunburn and skin cancer in SA's UV-intense environment. Heat sensitive. Requires shade and housing. Susceptible to parasites, especially Haemonchus. Requires high-quality nutrition to produce well.

Suitability: Intensive housed systems with shade provision. Cooler regions (Highveld, Western Cape, Southern Cape). Not suitable for extensive hot-climate systems.

Toggenburg

Origin: Toggenburg Valley, Switzerland. One of the oldest dairy goat breeds.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame. Brown with distinctive white facial stripes and lower legs. Does 50–65 kg. Erect ears.

Advantages: Good milk production (600–900 litres/lactation). Better pigmented than Saanen, providing some UV protection. Moderate nutritional requirements. Hardy for a dairy breed. Good for cheese making due to balanced fat-to-protein ratio.

Limitations: Lower peak yield than Saanen. Smaller gene pool in SA. Can be headstrong in temperament.

British Alpine & Crossbreeds

British Alpine: Black with white Swiss markings. Good production (700–1,000 litres). Better pigmented and hardier than Saanen. Small SA population.

SA crossbreeds: Crossing dairy breeds with Boer or indigenous goats creates animals with moderate milk production but greatly improved hardiness for SA conditions. This approach is practical for small-scale farmers wanting goat milk for household nutrition or small-scale sale.

Angora Goat (Mohair)

Origin & SA industry: Angora goats originated in the Ankara region of Turkey and were introduced to the Eastern Cape in the 1830s. South Africa is the world's largest producer of mohair, producing approximately 50% of global supply (2.5–3 million kg annually). The industry is concentrated in the Karoo and Eastern Cape. Mohair South Africa (previously the Mohair Board) manages industry affairs.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame. White, covered in lustrous, curly mohair fleece. Does 40–55 kg; bucks 65–85 kg. Ringlet-type fleece with defined lock structure.

Advantages: Mohair is a high-value luxury fibre used in suits, knitwear, and upholstery. SA mohair is internationally renowned for quality. Shearing occurs twice yearly (February and August), producing 3–6 kg mohair per shearing per adult. Kid mohair (from goats under 12 months) commands the highest price due to its fineness (≤26 micron). SA Mohair Growers' Association provides classification and marketing services.

Limitations: Extremely delicate — Angora goats are the least hardy of all goat types. Highly susceptible to cold stress (can die within hours of shearing in cold, wet weather — known as "exposure deaths"). Susceptible to parasites and heartwater. Require good nutrition (browse and supplementary feeding). Cannot tolerate the Lowveld or subtropical regions — heat and tick pressure are too severe. Abortion storms are common under stress.

Management essentials: Never shear before a cold front. Provide shelter for at least 14 days post-shearing. Shear kids for the first time at 6 months. Maintain strict parasite control. Protect from predators — Angora goats have very poor predator-avoidance instincts.

Suitability: Strictly the Karoo and semi-arid Eastern Cape. Not suited to high-rainfall, hot, or subtropical regions.

Cashmere potential: Some SA indigenous goats produce small amounts of cashmere (fine undercoat fibre). Research by the ARC and universities has explored cashmere production potential, but no commercial cashmere industry exists in SA yet. The Kalahari Red and some indigenous ecotypes show the most promise.

Parasites — The Number One Killer

Haemonchus contortus (barber's pole worm/wireworm): The single greatest cause of goat losses in SA. This blood-sucking stomach worm causes anaemia, bottle jaw (submandibular oedema), weakness, and death — especially in young kids and lactating does. Goats are more susceptible than sheep or cattle because they have never developed strong natural resistance (as browsers, they evolved with less worm exposure than grazers).

FAMACHA system: Developed in South Africa by Prof Faffa Malan and Prof Gareth Bath. A practical, on-farm anaemia-scoring system that uses the colour of the lower eyelid mucous membrane to identify worm-affected animals. Score 1 (red, healthy) to Score 5 (white, critically anaemic). Only deworm animals scoring 3–5. This targeted selective treatment (TST) slows the development of anthelmintic resistance and reduces costs. Check every 2–4 weeks during the wet season.

Anthelmintic resistance: A critical problem in SA goats. Many farms have worms resistant to all three major drug classes (benzimidazoles, levamisole, macrocyclic lactones). Combat resistance: use FAMACHA (don't blanket-deworm), weigh animals to dose correctly (goats metabolise drugs faster than sheep — use higher dose rates), rotate drug classes annually, and conduct faecal egg count reduction tests (FECRT) to check which drugs still work on your farm.

Diseases

Heartwater: Goats are highly susceptible to heartwater (Ehrlichia ruminantium) transmitted by the bont tick. Prevalent in bushveld and Lowveld. Mortality in naive goats can exceed 80%. Symptoms: high fever, nervous signs, recumbency. Treatment: oxytetracycline if caught early. Prevention: controlled tick exposure in young animals, tick control, and avoid moving goats from tick-free to tick-endemic areas without prophylaxis.

Pulpy kidney (enterotoxaemia): Caused by Clostridium perfringens type D. Kills quickly — often found dead with no warning. Vaccinate all goats with Multivax-P (pulpy kidney + tetanus) at weaning, booster 4 weeks later, then every 6 months. Always vaccinate does 4–6 weeks before kidding to ensure colostral immunity for kids.

Foot rot: Bacterial infection causing severe lameness, especially in wet conditions. Trim hooves regularly (every 6–8 weeks). Run goats through a 10% zinc sulphate or copper sulphate footbath. Affected animals need individual treatment with topical spray and systemic antibiotics.

Pasteurellosis (Pasteurella/Mannheimia): Respiratory disease triggered by stress (transport, shearing, cold weather). Vaccinate annually before the cold/wet season.

Housing, Predation & Kidding

Housing: Goats need shelter from rain (they detest getting wet) and wind. Raised slatted floors reduce parasite reinfection. Well-ventilated but draught-free. Minimum 1.5 m²/adult goat under cover. Separate pens for kidding, sick animals, and bucks.

Predator protection: Goats are highly vulnerable to predators (jackals, caracals, baboons, eagles on kids, stray dogs). Kraal overnight in a secure enclosure. Anatolian Karabash or similar livestock guardian dogs are highly effective. Jackal-proof fencing (1.2 m + electrified outrigger) for permanent camps. Never leave young kids unattended in open camps.

Kidding management: Gestation 148–152 days. Does typically kid twins (Boer goat twinning rate 60–80%). Ensure clean, dry kidding pens. Dip navels in 7% iodine solution immediately. Confirm each kid receives colostrum within 4 hours. Monitor does for retained afterbirth. Ring-castrate and disbud male kids at 3–7 days if not kept for breeding.

Sheep Farming in South Africa

South Africa has approximately 22 million sheep, making it one of the largest sheep-producing countries globally. The industry is divided between meat production (dominated by Dorper, Meatmaster, and fat-tailed breeds) and wool production (Merino and dual-purpose breeds). The Karoo, Free State, and Eastern Cape are the primary sheep-farming regions. South Africa is the world's 4th largest wool producer and has developed several internationally recognised breeds including the Dorper, Dohne Merino, Meatmaster, and Dormer. Cape Wools SA manages wool industry affairs, while the National Wool Growers' Association (NWGA) represents producer interests.

Dorper

Origin & history: South Africa's most successful sheep breed export, developed in the 1940s by the Department of Agriculture from Dorset Horn × Blackhead Persian crosses. Bred to produce a hardy, non-wool meat sheep for the arid Karoo. The Dorper Sheep Breeders' Society of SA is one of the largest sheep societies in the world. Now farmed in over 40 countries.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame. White body with black head (Dorper) or all-white (White Dorper). Ewes 60–80 kg; rams 90–120 kg. Short, loose hair/wool mixture that sheds naturally (no shearing required). Well-muscled, barrel-shaped body.

Advantages: Exceptional hardiness — thrives from the Kalahari desert to the Highveld. Self-shedding — eliminates shearing costs. Non-selective grazers — utilise a wide range of plants. Outstanding adaptability to heat and cold. Good fertility (lambing percentage 120–160%). Fast growth (ADG 200–300 g). Good carcass quality with even fat distribution. Low maintenance requirements. Long breeding season — can breed throughout the year.

Limitations: Fat cover can become excessive on high-nutrition feedlot diets. Produces no wool income. Hogget and mutton can be slightly coarser than wool-breed equivalents.

Suitability: The most versatile sheep breed in SA. Suited to virtually all regions and farming systems — communal, extensive, semi-intensive. The recommended breed for beginners.

Meatmaster

Origin: SA-developed composite breed from Damara, Dorper, Van Rooy, and other fat-tailed and hair-type breeds. Breed society established in 2008. Rapidly growing in popularity.

Physical characteristics: Medium to large frame. Any colour (breed selects on function, not appearance). Ewes 60–85 kg; rams 90–130 kg. Hair-type coat that sheds naturally. Fat-tailed or fat-rumped.

Advantages: Bred for "total efficiency" — maximum kg lamb weaned per kg ewe maintained. Outstanding hardiness. Excellent mothering — ewes milk well and lambs grow rapidly. Good walking ability. Adaptable to arid conditions. Fat tail/rump serves as energy reserve during drought. Self-shedding coat.

Limitations: Relatively new breed — less standardised than Dorper. Fat tail may be undesirable in some formal market channels. Variable phenotype in early generations.

Suitability: Arid and semi-arid regions. Excellent for extensive, low-input farming systems. Growing role in communal farming development projects.

Van Rooy

Origin: Developed by Senator J.C. van Rooy in the Free State from Ronderib Afrikaner and imported Blinkvlei-Ronderib crosses in the early 1900s. A fat-tailed meat breed.

Physical characteristics: Large frame, all white. Ewes 60–80 kg; rams 90–120 kg. Distinctive fat rump/tail. Bare head. Hairy coat that sheds.

Advantages: Very hardy. Excellent fertility. Good milk production. Fat rump acts as energy reserve. Self-shedding.

Damara

Origin: Indigenous fat-tailed sheep from Namibia/southern Angola. One of the oldest sheep breeds in southern Africa, kept by the Damara people for centuries.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame. Variable colours — brown, black, white, or multicoloured. Ewes 40–60 kg; rams 60–80 kg. Prominent fat tail. Hair coat. Agile and athletic.

Advantages: Maximum hardiness — the ultimate survivor sheep. Exceptional predator awareness and flock instinct. Good mothering. Self-shedding. Thrives on minimal inputs. Cultural value in some communities.

Limitations: Small carcass. Slow growth. Fat tail. Low commercial value per animal.

Other Meat Breeds

Blackhead Persian: Indigenous fat-tailed breed from Somalia/East Africa. Small frame (ewes 35–45 kg). Extremely hardy. Important in communal farming. Basis breed for Dorper development.

Ile de France: French breed. Large frame, heavily muscled. Excellent terminal sire for producing meaty lambs. Ewes 70–90 kg; rams 100–130 kg. Requires good nutrition and management.

Suffolk: English breed, popular as a terminal sire. Black face and legs, white fleece. Exceptional muscling and lean meat. Birth weights can be high — use over mature ewes only to avoid dystocia. Ewes 70–90 kg; rams 100–140 kg.

Texel: Dutch breed. Outstanding muscling, especially hindquarter. Very lean carcass. Excellent terminal sire. Moderate adaptation to SA conditions.

Merino

Origin & SA industry: Originated in Spain, refined in Australia and SA. Introduced to the Cape in 1789. South Africa's Merino flock of approximately 8 million sheep is the world's 4th largest, producing some of the finest apparel wool globally. Cape Wools SA oversees clip preparation, classification, and marketing. SA Merino wool clips range from 16.5 to 24 micron, with ultra-fine clips commanding premium international prices.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame. White, covered entirely in dense, fine wool. Ewes 50–70 kg; rams 80–110 kg. Some strains are horned (rams), others polled. Fine-boned, wrinkle-free modern types (smooth-body Merino) are preferred.

Advantages: Wool is a high-value, renewable fibre harvested annually. Annual fleece weight 4–8 kg, depending on strain and nutrition. Fine wool (≤19 micron) commands premium prices. Excellent fertility when managed well. Adaptable to both arid (Karoo) and wetter (grassland) environments. Good longevity. Dual income stream (wool + meat) provides financial stability — wool income during lean times offsets meat price volatility.

Wool classes: Clip preparation follows strict Cape Wools SA guidelines. Wool is classified by fineness (micron), length (mm), strength (N/ktex), vegetable matter content, and colour. Well-prepared clips with separate lines (fleece, bellies, pieces, crutchings) attract higher prices. Shear annually in spring (September–October in most regions).

Limitations: Requires shearing (annual cost). Susceptible to blowfly strike (especially in wet conditions). Susceptible to footrot. Lower meat conformation than dedicated meat breeds. Requires skilled management and husbandry.

Suitability: Karoo (producing the finest wool), Free State, and Eastern Cape grasslands. Suited to experienced sheep farmers with access to wool marketing infrastructure.

Dohne Merino

Origin: Developed in South Africa at the Dohne Research Station near Stutterheim, Eastern Cape, from Merino × German Mutton Merino crosses in the 1930s–1950s. A true dual-purpose breed delivering both quality wool and marketable lamb carcasses. Now farmed in over 20 countries.

Physical characteristics: Medium to large frame. White, plain-bodied, polled. Ewes 55–75 kg; rams 90–120 kg. Good muscling over the loin and hindquarter combined with fine to medium wool (19–22 micron).

Advantages: Unique combination of Merino-quality wool (fleece 3.5–5.5 kg) with excellent meat conformation. Lambs grow fast enough for slaughter markets. Good mothering and milk production — weaning weights higher than pure Merino. Adaptable to a wide range of environments. Good walking ability. Excellent breed for farmers wanting both wool and meat income without compromising on either.

Limitations: Neither the finest wool producer (Merino is better) nor the heaviest meat producer (Dorper/Ile de France are better). Requires balanced management for both wool and meat production.

Suitability: Widely suited across SA. Particularly valuable in the Eastern Cape, KZN Midlands, and grassland areas where dual-income farming is desirable.

SA Mutton Merino (SAMM)

Origin: Developed in SA from German Mutton Merino imports. A meat-type merino with heavier body and coarser wool than the fine-wool Merino.

Physical characteristics: Large frame. White. Ewes 65–85 kg; rams 100–130 kg. Good muscling. Medium-strong wool (22–26 micron). Polled.

Advantages: Excellent lamb growth rates. Heavy carcass. Good wool clip. Good mothering. Versatile dual-purpose. Well-established breed in SA.

Dormer

Origin: SA-developed from Dorset Horn × Merino crosses at Elsenburg Research Station, Western Cape, in the 1940s.

Physical characteristics: Large frame, white. Ewes 65–85 kg; rams 95–130 kg. Excellent muscling, similar to Dorset Horn. Medium wool. Polled.

Advantages: Outstanding carcass conformation. Fast lamb growth. Good wool production. Extended breeding season (Dorset Horn influence). Good mothering. Excellent terminal sire over Merino ewes to produce quality slaughter lambs while maintaining wool flock.

Suitability: Widely adapted across SA. Excellent terminal sire in wool-flock crossbreeding programmes.

Predator Management

Scale of the problem: Predation is the most significant challenge facing SA sheep farmers, costing the industry an estimated R1.4 billion annually. Jackals (black-backed jackal), caracals, leopards, stray/feral dogs, and baboons are the primary predators. Lambs are most vulnerable in the first 2 weeks of life.

Jackal-proof fencing: The gold standard for predator exclusion. 1.2 m high stock fencing with an additional 3–4 strand electrified outrigger (alternating positive/negative, lowest wire 15 cm above fence). Apron wire at the base prevents digging. Expensive to install (R25,000–R40,000/km) but highly effective. Maintain regularly — a single gap invites predators in.

Livestock guardian animals: Anatolian Karabash dogs are the most popular and effective. Raise pups with lambs from 8 weeks of age to bond with sheep. One dog per 100–200 ewes. Alpacas and donkeys also deter predators but are less effective than dogs. Collared vultures/raptors should never be harmed — they are protected species.

Management practices: Kraal sheep at night (especially during lambing). Concentrate lambing in small, well-fenced camps near the homestead. Remove afterbirth and dead animals promptly — they attract predators. Never use poison (illegal under many circumstances and kills non-target species including vultures).

Internal Parasites

Wireworm (Haemonchus): Major killer, especially in summer-rainfall areas. Use FAMACHA scoring for targeted treatment. Maintain refugia — leave some animals undosed so that susceptible (non-resistant) worm genes remain in the population. Smart drenching reduces resistance development.

Blowfly strike: Lucilia cuprina lays eggs in soiled, moist wool — particularly around the crutch (breech) and any wounds. Maggots hatch and literally eat the sheep alive. Prevention: crutch (shear breech area) before fly season; apply preventative pour-on (e.g., cyromazine); select for wrinkle-free, plain-bodied sheep; mules-less breeding (SRS — Soft Rolling Skin type selection).

Diseases & Vaccination

Pulpy kidney (Clostridium perfringens type D): The most important sheep vaccination in SA. Kills rapidly — often found dead. Vaccinate all sheep with Multivax-P at weaning + booster 4 weeks later, then every 6 months. Vaccinate ewes 4–6 weeks before lambing.

Blue tongue: Viral disease spread by Culicoides midges. Causes fever, swollen tongue, mouth ulceration, foot lesions, and death. Endemic in many SA regions. Vaccinate with OBP polyvalent vaccine annually before the midge season (summer). Multiple serotypes exist — vaccine covers the most common.

Rift Valley fever: Mosquito-borne viral disease causing abortion storms in pregnant ewes and high mortality in lambs. Outbreaks follow heavy summer rains. Vaccinate during outbreaks as directed by DALRRD. Zoonotic — wear gloves when handling affected animals.

Scab (sheep scab — Psoroptes ovis): Notifiable disease causing intense itching, wool loss, and hide damage. Compulsory dipping in some provinces. Report suspected cases to state veterinarian. Treat with injectable ivermectin or plunge dipping.

Footrot (Dichelobacter nodosus): Bacterial infection causing severe lameness in wet conditions. Prevention: regular hoof trimming, zinc sulphate foot baths, culling chronic carriers. Avoid buying sheep from affected farms.

Breeding & Lambing

Flushing: Increase ewe nutrition 2–3 weeks before and 2–3 weeks after ram introduction. Moving ewes to fresh, higher-quality grazing or supplementing with 200–300 g grain/day increases ovulation rate and twinning percentage by 10–20%. Ewes should be in rising body condition (BCS 3.0–3.5 on a 5-point scale) at mating.

Ram:ewe ratio: 1:30 to 1:40 for mature rams. 1:15 to 1:20 for young rams (first season). Use raddle harnesses or crayon markers to track mating — change colour every 17 days to identify ewes returning to service. Conduct BSE on all rams before the season.

Lambing management: Move ewes to clean, sheltered lambing camps 2–4 weeks before expected lambing. First-time ewes (maidens) need closer supervision. Ensure ewes have adequate nutrition in late pregnancy — 70% of foetal growth occurs in the last 6 weeks. Under-nutrition causes pregnancy toxaemia (twin-lamb disease) and weak lambs.

Lamb survival: The first 72 hours are critical. Ensure lambs suckle within 1 hour. Hypothermia and starvation (often combined) are the leading causes of neonatal lamb death. Orphan lambs can be fostered onto ewes that lost lambs (wet-adopt, skin grafting) or reared on lamb milk replacer. Target lamb survival >90%.

Weaning & Shearing

Weaning: Wean lambs at 90–120 days (3–4 months). Dorper lambs: target 30–40 kg at weaning; Merino lambs: 25–35 kg. Place weaned lambs on the best available grazing/feed. Early weaning (60–70 days) reduces ewe nutritional demand and allows ewes to recover body condition before the next mating.

Shearing (wool breeds): Shear once annually, typically in spring (September–October) before summer heat. Use professional shearers trained in correct technique to minimise second cuts and skin damage. Follow Cape Wools SA clip preparation standards: separate fleece, bellies, pieces, locks, crutchings, and stained wool into separate bales. A well-prepared clip can earn 10–20% more than a poorly prepared one. Provide shelter for freshly shorn sheep for at least 7–14 days (sunburn and cold exposure risk).

Pig Farming in South Africa

South Africa's pig industry produces approximately 250,000 tonnes of pork annually from a national herd of around 1.5 million pigs. The industry is dominated by large commercial operations in the Free State, North West, Western Cape, and Limpopo, but there is significant growth potential for smallholder producers. Pigs are monogastric (non-ruminant) animals with fast reproduction rates and efficient feed conversion, making them ideal for intensive farming. The South African Pork Producers' Organisation (SAPPO) represents the industry, and pork safety is assured through the Pork360 quality assurance programme.

Large White (Yorkshire)

Origin: Yorkshire, England. The most widely used breed in the SA pig industry.

Physical characteristics: Large frame, all white with erect ears. Sows 200–280 kg; boars 280–370 kg. Long body, good depth.

Advantages: Outstanding maternal traits — large litters (12–14 born alive), good milk production, excellent mothering. Versatile as both dam and sire line. Good growth rate (ADG 750–900 g). Good carcass quality. Docile temperament. Well-established genetics in SA with extensive performance data.

Limitations: White skin susceptible to sunburn in outdoor systems. Moderate lean meat percentage compared to specialised sire lines.

Suitability: All production systems. The standard dam-line breed in SA. Used in virtually every commercial piggery.

SA Landrace

Origin: Developed from Scandinavian Landrace imports, adapted to SA conditions over decades of local selection.

Physical characteristics: Large frame, all white with large, drooping ears. Sows 200–280 kg; boars 270–350 kg. Very long body (extra vertebrae give additional rib/loin chops). Fine skin.

Advantages: Exceptional litter size (13–15 born alive in top herds). Excellent milk production. Very long carcass — extra loin length means more high-value cuts. Good ham development. The standard dam-line breed used alternately or in combination with Large White.

Limitations: Slightly lower growth rate than Large White. Sensitive to stress. Susceptible to leg weakness (osteochondrosis) in heavy animals. Sunburn risk in outdoor systems.

Suitability: Indoor intensive systems. Almost always used as a dam line, frequently crossed with Large White (F1 crossbred sow is the industry standard).

Duroc

Origin: USA (New Jersey/New York). Introduced to SA as a sire-line breed.

Physical characteristics: Large frame, red/cherry-red colour with drooping ears. Boars 280–370 kg. Well-muscled, deep body.

Advantages: Fastest growth rate (ADG 850–1,000 g) and best feed conversion (FCR 2.2–2.7:1) of common breeds. Outstanding meat quality with superior intramuscular fat (marbling) — produces the best-eating pork. Good stress resistance. Good outdoor hardiness due to pigmented skin. Excellent terminal sire.

Limitations: Smaller litter size (9–11 born alive) — not suited as a dam line. Carcass can be fatter if nutrition not managed. Red pigment can cause hide discolouration concerns in some markets.

Suitability: Exclusively as a terminal sire line over Large White × Landrace crossbred sows. Also suited to outdoor/free-range production due to skin pigmentation.

Pietrain

Origin: Belgium. Extreme muscling breed.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame, white with black spots (piebald). Extremely heavy muscling, especially in the ham. Boars 260–320 kg.

Advantages: Highest lean meat percentage of any breed (62–67% lean). Exceptional ham conformation. Outstanding for producing lean carcasses.

Limitations: Highly susceptible to Porcine Stress Syndrome (PSS — halothane gene). PSS causes sudden death under stress, pale/soft/exudative (PSE) meat, and poor meat quality. Lower growth rate. Smaller litters. Requires careful genetic testing and management. Not widely available in SA.

Suitability: Specialist sire line in high-lean production systems. Only halothane-negative tested animals should be used.

Hampshire

Origin: England, refined in the USA. Recognisable by its black body with white shoulder belt.

Physical characteristics: Large frame, black with white belt around shoulders and front legs. Well-muscled, lean. Boars 270–350 kg.

Advantages: Excellent lean meat production. Good growth rate. Active, vigorous animals. Good as a terminal sire line for lean carcasses. Hardy.

Limitations: Some lines carry the "Hampshire effect" — high glycolytic potential causing poor meat quality under certain conditions. Smaller gene pool in SA.

Kolbroek

Origin: Indigenous SA breed, developed over centuries by communal farmers from early Chinese and European pig introductions. Named after the Kolbroek area in Mpumalanga. One of very few truly indigenous pig breeds in Africa.

Physical characteristics: Small to medium frame. Black, occasionally black-and-white. Sows 60–100 kg; boars 80–120 kg. Short snout, small ears, compact body. Heavy backfat.

Advantages: Maximum hardiness — survives on forage, kitchen waste, and minimal commercial feed. Excellent disease resistance. Good mothering instinct. Tolerant of heat and tick-borne diseases. Very low input requirements. Good for outdoor/free-range systems. Important genetic resource for communal food security. Produces flavourful meat with good fat cover.

Limitations: Very slow growth (reaching 60–80 kg in 8–12 months vs. 5–6 months for commercial breeds). High backfat — does not meet modern lean-carcass market specifications. Small carcass. Low feed conversion efficiency compared to commercial breeds.

Suitability: Communal and subsistence farming systems where inputs are minimal. Heritage/niche meat markets valuing flavour over leanness. Outdoor and free-range systems. Genetic conservation programmes.

Other breeds: Chester White — white breed used as dam line, good mothering, moderate in SA. Large Black — heritage breed suited to outdoor/free-range systems, black pigmented skin, good foraging ability, gaining interest in niche SA markets.

Intensive (indoor) systems: The standard in SA commercial production. Fully enclosed climate-controlled houses. Concrete or slatted floors with slurry channels beneath. Sections: dry sow house (individual stalls or group housing), farrowing house (farrowing crates with heated creep areas for piglets), weaner house (post-weaning to 25–30 kg), and grower/finisher house (30 kg to slaughter at 90–110 kg). Minimum space: 0.7 m²/weaner, 1.0 m²/grower, 1.5 m²/finisher, 2.4 m²/dry sow (group), 5 m² farrowing crate + creep.

Outdoor/free-range systems: Growing niche market in SA. Requires well-drained land. Individual farrowing huts (arks) for sows. Paddock rotation prevents soil degradation and parasite buildup. Pigmented breeds (Duroc, Kolbroek, Large Black) essential — white breeds suffer severe sunburn. Fencing must be robust (pigs are strong diggers). Lower capital cost but higher management input. Stocking rate: 15–25 sows/ha with rotation.

Ventilation: Critical for pig health. Pigs produce large amounts of moisture, ammonia, and carbon dioxide. Ammonia above 25 ppm causes respiratory disease and reduced growth. Ventilation systems: natural (open-sided houses with curtains) or mechanical (fans and inlets). Target: 0.7 m³/min airflow per finishing pig in summer; minimum 0.1 m³/min in winter.

Waste management: A pig produces 5–8% of its body weight in manure and urine daily. Manage via: slurry systems (flushed or scraped to lagoons), composting, or biogas digesters. Apply slurry to cropland at agronomic rates (avoid nutrient overloading). Comply with NEMA waste management regulations. Odour management — locate piggeries downwind of residences and maintain aerobic treatment systems.

Biosecurity: Single most important management principle. Single-age, all-in-all-out production per house. Boundary fencing to exclude feral pigs and wildlife. Footbaths and overalls at entry. Vehicle wash stations. Quarantine period for new stock (minimum 30 days). Dead pig disposal (incineration or burial with lime). Visitor log and restricted access. Biosecurity is the primary defence against African Swine Fever.

Feed cost: Represents 60–70% of total pig production cost. Ration formulation by a qualified nutritionist is essential — even small imbalances waste money and reduce performance.

Feeding phases:

Creep feed (7–28 days): Highly digestible, high-protein (22%) starter. Introduces piglets to solid feed while still on sow. Small quantities, replaced frequently.

Weaner (28–70 days, 7–25 kg): 19–20% crude protein, 14 MJ DE/kg. Feeds must be palatable and digestible — feed intake post-weaning is critical. Zinc oxide supplementation (first 2 weeks post-weaning) helps prevent scours.

Grower (70–120 days, 25–60 kg): 17–18% crude protein, 13.5 MJ DE/kg. Ad-lib feeding for maximum growth.

Finisher (120–160 days, 60–110 kg): 15–16% crude protein, 13.0 MJ DE/kg. May restrict-feed in final 2–3 weeks to control backfat.

Sow lactation: 17–18% crude protein, 14 MJ DE/kg. Feed ad-lib — a lactating sow needs 6–10 kg feed/day. Under-feeding reduces milk, piglet growth, and subsequent fertility.

Feed conversion ratio (FCR): Target 2.3–2.8:1 from weaning to slaughter. Top operations achieve below 2.3:1. FCR is the key driver of profitability.

Alternative feeds: Bakery waste, brewery grains, fruit pulp, and oil press cake can partially replace maize/soy at lower cost. Must be nutritionally analysed and free from mycotoxins. Swill feeding (kitchen waste containing meat) is ILLEGAL in SA due to African Swine Fever risk.

African Swine Fever (ASF)

The single greatest threat to SA pig farming. ASF is a devastating, highly contagious viral disease with near-100% mortality in domestic pigs. There is NO vaccine and NO cure. It is a controlled disease under the Animal Diseases Act. Outbreaks occur regularly in SA — particularly in the northern provinces near wild warthog populations (which carry the virus without showing symptoms) and in areas where biosecurity is poor.

Transmission: Direct contact with infected pigs, contaminated feed/vehicles/equipment, soft ticks (Ornithodoros), or feeding contaminated pork products (swill). Virus survives for months in meat products, blood, and the environment.

Prevention: Strict biosecurity is the ONLY defence. No contact between domestic pigs and warthogs/bushpigs. No swill feeding (illegal). No sharing of equipment between farms. Quarantine all new pigs for 30+ days. Report any suspected case immediately to your state veterinarian (sudden deaths, high fever, blue/red skin discolouration, bloody diarrhoea). Early detection and containment are critical.

Other Important Diseases

Porcine Reproductive and Respiratory Syndrome (PRRS): SA is currently considered PRRS-free, which is a significant competitive advantage. Import controls and biosecurity maintain this status. Characterised by late-term abortions, respiratory disease, and increased mortality. Surveillance is ongoing.

Swine influenza: Causes coughing, fever, and reduced feed intake. Usually self-limiting but reduces growth rate. Good ventilation and avoiding stress reduce severity.

Porcine circovirus (PCV2): Causes post-weaning multi-systemic wasting syndrome (PMWS). Vaccinate at weaning. Present in SA herds.

E. coli scours (neonatal and post-weaning): Leading cause of pre-weaning and post-weaning mortality. Maintain farrowing and weaner house hygiene. Sow vaccination pre-farrowing. Zinc oxide post-weaning. Avoid abrupt feed changes.

Internal parasites: Ascaris suum (roundworm) causes liver condemnation at slaughter and reduced growth. Deworm sows before farrowing and routinely in extensive systems. Intensive slatted-floor systems have lower parasite burden.

Poultry Farming in South Africa

Poultry is South Africa's largest agricultural sub-sector by value, producing over 1.8 million tonnes of broiler meat and 500,000 tonnes of eggs annually. The industry is the most accessible entry point into commercial livestock production — operations can start small (even 100 birds) and scale rapidly. The South African Poultry Association (SAPA) represents both the broiler and egg industries. Poultry farming is critical for food security, employment, and rural economic development. South Africa also has a rich heritage of indigenous chicken breeds developed by the ARC.

Commercial Strains

Ross 308: The most widely used broiler strain in SA. Known for robust growth, good feed conversion, and excellent liveability. Target: 2.2 kg live weight at 35 days with FCR 1.5–1.6. Bred by Aviagen (distributed by Ross Poultry Breeders in SA).

Cobb 500: Strong competitor to Ross 308. Noted for uniformity and breast meat yield. Performs well in SA conditions. Target: 2.1–2.3 kg at 35 days. Slightly higher breast percentage than Ross 308.

Management

Brooding (days 0–14): Maintain house temperature at 33–34°C at chick level on day 1, reducing by 0.5°C/day to 24°C by day 21. Use infrared lamps, gas brooders, or hot-water heating. Paper on litter for first 3 days aids feed finding. 23 hours light on day 1, reducing to 18 hours by day 7. Fresh, clean water from arrival — dehydration in the first 24 hours causes permanent growth setback.

Stocking density: 10–12 birds/m² for open-sided houses in SA; up to 18–20 birds/m² in fully climate-controlled (tunnel-ventilated) houses. Higher densities require superior ventilation and management. SAPA/NSPCA welfare guidelines specify maximum densities of 35–38 kg live weight/m² at slaughter.

Litter management: Use wood shavings or sunflower husks at 8–10 cm depth. Keep litter dry (target moisture 20–25%). Wet litter causes ammonia release, footpad dermatitis, and respiratory disease. Top up or replace wet patches. Litter temperature at day 1 should be 28–30°C (pre-heat house 24–48 hours before chick placement).

Feeding

Starter (days 0–10): 23% crude protein, 12.5 MJ ME/kg. Crumble form for easy consumption. Contains coccidiostat.

Grower (days 11–24): 21% crude protein, 13.0 MJ ME/kg. Pellet or crumble.

Finisher (days 25 to slaughter): 19% crude protein, 13.2 MJ ME/kg. Pellet form. Withdraw coccidiostat and any medications according to withdrawal periods before slaughter.

Feed cost: Represents 65–72% of total broiler production cost. Feed conversion ratio (FCR) is the key profit driver — target 1.5–1.7:1 at 35 days. Every 0.1 improvement in FCR saves approximately R0.30–R0.50/bird.

Target performance: 2.0–2.4 kg live weight at 35 days. Daily weight gain 55–65 g/day. Mortality below 4%. European Production Efficiency Factor (EPEF) above 350 is considered good; top SA operations exceed 400.

Ventilation

Open-sided houses: Most common in SA for small-to-medium operations. Side curtains (manually or automatically controlled) regulate airflow. Orient house east-west for shade. Minimum width 12m, maximum 15m. Install ceiling and end-wall fans for summer.

Tunnel-ventilated houses: Large commercial standard. Air enters through evaporative cooling pads at one end and is extracted by exhaust fans at the other. Provides wind-chill cooling of 5–8°C. Essential for densities above 14 birds/m². Capital-intensive but dramatically improves performance in hot SA summers.

Commercial Strains

Hy-Line Brown: Widely used in SA. Brown-egg layer. Robust, good liveability. Peak production 94–96% at 25–30 weeks. 330–340 eggs per hen housed to 80 weeks. FCR 2.0–2.1 kg feed/kg eggs.

Lohmann Brown: Another popular brown-egg strain. Similar performance to Hy-Line. Known for persistent production. 330–340 eggs to 80 weeks.

Amberlink: Developed in SA by Hy-Line. Dual-purpose qualities — slightly heavier body for end-of-lay cull value. Good for free-range and small-scale production due to hardiness.

Housing Systems

Conventional cages: Being phased out in many markets due to welfare concerns. High density (450–550 cm²/bird). Lowest production cost per egg. Being replaced by enriched cages in progressive operations.

Enriched/colony cages: Larger cages with perches, nest areas, and scratching pads. 750+ cm²/bird. Balance between welfare and production efficiency. Growing adoption in SA.

Barn/free-range: Floor housing at 7–9 birds/m² indoors with outdoor access (free-range: minimum 1 m²/bird outdoors). Premium egg prices (20–40% above cage eggs). Higher labour, predation, and disease management requirements. Nesting boxes at 1 per 5–7 hens. Perches at 15–18 cm/bird.

Lighting & Egg Grading

Lighting programmes: Pullets reared on decreasing day length (16 hours at day 1, decreasing to 10 hours by 10 weeks). From 16–18 weeks, increase light by 30 minutes/week to stimulate onset of lay. Maintain 16 hours of light once laying begins — never decrease during lay. Light intensity: 10–30 lux at bird level. Consistent lighting is essential for consistent production.

Egg grading: SA eggs are graded by the Perishable Products Export Control Board (PPECB) standards. Grades: Super Jumbo (>73 g), Jumbo (66–73 g), Extra Large (59–66 g), Large (52–59 g), Medium (45–52 g), Small (38–45 g). Shell quality, cleanliness, and internal quality (Haugh units) determine grade. Cool eggs to below 20°C within 24 hours. Shelf life: 35 days from lay date under SA regulations.

Potchefstroom Koekoek

Origin: Developed by the ARC at Potchefstroom Research Station by crossing White Leghorn, Black Australorp, and Barred Plymouth Rock. The most popular dual-purpose indigenous breed in SA.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame. Barred (black-and-white striped) plumage — sex-linkable at day old (males lighter barring). Hens 2.5–3.5 kg; roosters 3.5–4.5 kg.

Advantages: Excellent dual-purpose — produces 200–250 eggs/year (brown, medium-sized) while also providing a reasonable meat carcass. Hardy, disease resistant, good forager. Ideal for free-range and small-scale communal farming. Auto-sexing at hatch (colour difference between male and female chicks) simplifies flock management. Broody — can hatch own replacements without incubators. ARC maintains the parent stock.

Limitations: Lower egg production than commercial strains. Slower growth than commercial broilers. Broodiness reduces laying time.

Suitability: The recommended breed for communal, small-scale, and household food-security projects across SA. Free-range and organic production. Excellent for rural livelihood programmes.

Ovambo

Origin: Indigenous breed from the Ovambo people of northern Namibia/southern Angola. One of the smallest SA chicken breeds.

Physical characteristics: Small frame. Variable colours — often black, speckled, or red. Hens 1.2–1.8 kg; roosters 1.8–2.5 kg. Alert, upright posture. Excellent fliers.

Advantages: Maximum hardiness and disease resistance. Excellent predator avoidance (flighty, roosts in trees). Good brooding instinct. Survives on minimal feed through scavenging. Adapted to hot, arid conditions.

Limitations: Very small carcass and low egg production (60–100 eggs/year). Flighty and difficult to manage in confinement.

Venda

Origin: Indigenous breed from the Venda people of Limpopo.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame. Colourful — multicoloured plumage including brown, black, white, and speckled. Hens 1.5–2.5 kg.

Advantages: Good forager. Hardy and disease resistant. Good brooding and mothering. Culturally significant in Venda traditions. Better egg production than Ovambo (80–150 eggs/year).

Naked Neck

Origin: Found across Africa, Asia, and Eastern Europe. Also called Turken due to turkey-like bare neck.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame. Bare neck (featherless, exposed red skin). Various colours. Hens 2.0–3.0 kg.

Advantages: Superior heat tolerance due to reduced feather cover (up to 40% fewer feathers). Excellent for hot SA regions. Better feed efficiency than fully feathered breeds in hot weather (less energy wasted on heat dissipation). Good egg production for an indigenous type (120–180 eggs/year). Easier to pluck.

Boschveld

Origin: Developed by the ARC as a three-way composite from Venda, Ovambo, and Matabele indigenous breeds. Named after the Boschveld region in Limpopo.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame, reddish-brown plumage. Hens 2.0–3.0 kg.

Advantages: Combines the hardiness and disease resistance of three indigenous lines. Better production than any single indigenous parent (150–200 eggs/year). Good forager, free-range adaptability. Hybrid vigour. Growing commercial production for free-range markets.

Newcastle Disease (NDV)

The most devastating poultry disease in SA. Newcastle disease virus (NDV) is highly contagious and can kill 80–100% of an unvaccinated flock within days. Velogenic (highly virulent) strains are endemic in SA. Symptoms: sudden high mortality, respiratory distress (gasping, coughing), greenish diarrhoea, drooping wings, twisted neck (torticollis), drop in egg production.

Vaccination is NON-NEGOTIABLE: Day 1: spray/eye drop with live LaSota or Clone 30 vaccine. Day 14–18: booster with live LaSota (drinking water). Week 8–10: killed oil-emulsion NDV vaccine (injection — provides long-term immunity). Layers: booster killed vaccine at 16–18 weeks. Revaccinate with live vaccine every 6–8 weeks during lay. Communal/backyard flocks: vaccinate with I-2 thermotolerant vaccine (available from ARC — designed for village poultry, heat stable).

Other Critical Diseases

Highly Pathogenic Avian Influenza (HPAI): SA experienced devastating H5N8 outbreaks in 2017 affecting over 2 million birds. HPAI is a controlled disease — any suspected case must be reported immediately. Biosecurity, surveillance, and rapid response are the primary controls. Vaccination against HPAI is not routinely practised in SA.

Infectious Bursal Disease (Gumboro/IBD): Destroys the bursa of Fabricius (immune organ), leaving chicks immunocompromised and susceptible to all other diseases. Vaccinate at 10–14 days and 21–24 days (timing depends on maternal antibody levels — perform ELISA testing for optimal scheduling).

Marek's Disease: Herpesvirus causing tumours, paralysis, and mortality in growing birds. Vaccinate at the hatchery on day 1 (in ovo or subcutaneous). Vaccination is standard in all commercial hatcheries. No treatment — prevention only.

Coccidiosis: Protozoal gut disease (Eimeria species) causing bloody diarrhoea, poor growth, and mortality. Controlled via: in-feed coccidiostats (ionophores or chemicals) in broiler feed; or live coccidiosis vaccine sprayed on day-old chicks (allows development of natural immunity — used in layers and long-life birds). Litter management is critical — wet litter promotes coccidiosis cycling.

Biosecurity Essentials

Between flocks (all-in-all-out): Remove all birds and litter. Wash house with high-pressure water and detergent. Disinfect with approved disinfectant (quaternary ammonium, glutaraldehyde, or formaldehyde fumigation). Rest period of 10–14 days minimum before new flock placement. This break cycle is critical for disease control.

During production: Restrict farm access. Footbaths at each house entrance. Dedicated clothing and boots per house. No contact with backyard/village poultry. Control wild birds, rodents, and insects (disease vectors). Dispose of dead birds immediately (composting, incineration, or deep burial with lime). Monitor daily mortality — any sudden increase is an emergency signal.

Rabbit Farming in South Africa

Rabbit farming (cuniculture) is an emerging but growing sector in South Africa, driven by demand for lean, healthy protein. Rabbit meat is exceptionally nutritious — high in protein (21%), low in fat (5%), low in cholesterol, and easily digestible. Rabbits are efficient converters of plant material to protein, require minimal space, and reproduce rapidly, making them ideal for small-scale and urban farming. The South African Rabbit Breeders Association promotes the industry.

New Zealand White

Origin: USA. The world's most popular commercial meat rabbit breed. Widely used in SA.

Physical characteristics: Medium to large frame. All white with red/pink eyes (albino). Adults 4.0–5.5 kg. Compact, well-muscled body with excellent loin and hindquarter development.

Advantages: Fast growth — fryers reach 2.0–2.5 kg at 8–10 weeks. Excellent meat-to-bone ratio. Good feed conversion (3.0–3.5:1). Docile temperament. Good litter size (8–10 kits). Good mothering. White fur is commercially useful (dyes easily). Well-suited to SA conditions.

Limitations: Albino eyes sensitive to bright light — provide shade. White fur shows soiling easily.

Californian

Origin: USA (California). The second most popular commercial meat breed.

Physical characteristics: Medium frame. White body with black points (nose, ears, feet, tail). Adults 3.5–5.0 kg. Compact, meaty body.

Advantages: Excellent carcass quality with fine bone structure — high meat yield percentage. Good growth rate. Docile. Good in crossbreeding programmes with New Zealand White (F1 crosses show hybrid vigour with improved litter size and growth).

Flemish Giant

Origin: Belgium. The largest domestic rabbit breed.

Physical characteristics: Very large frame. Various colours — steel grey, fawn, black, white. Adults 6.0–10.0 kg (some exceed 12 kg). Long body, large head.

Advantages: Used primarily as a crossbreeding sire to increase size in commercial programmes. Impressive appearance attracts interest. Gentle temperament for its size.

Limitations: Slow growth rate relative to size (late maturity — 12+ months). Higher feed consumption. Lower feed conversion efficiency than NZW. Higher bone-to-meat ratio. More prone to sore hocks (pododermatitis) due to weight on wire flooring.

Other Breeds

Chinchilla: Medium frame (4–5 kg). Attractive grey fur resembling chinchilla. Good dual-purpose (meat and fur). Popular with hobbyists.

Rex: Medium frame (3.5–5 kg). Distinctive short, plush, velvet-like fur. Primarily a fur breed but produces acceptable carcass. Rex pelts are the most valuable of any rabbit breed.

SA commercial crossbreeds: Most SA commercial rabbitries use NZW × Californian crosses, sometimes incorporating Flemish Giant. Crossbreeding provides hybrid vigour — 5–10% improvement in litter size and growth rate over purebreds.

Housing

Cage systems: Galvanised wire mesh cages raised 80–100 cm off the ground on legs or suspended from frames. Individual cages for does: minimum 0.5 m² floor space (0.75 m² for giant breeds), 45 cm height. Buck cages: 0.4–0.5 m². Grower cages: 0.07 m²/rabbit. Wire mesh floor (12 × 12 mm or 12 × 75 mm for adults; 12 × 12 mm for kits) allows droppings to fall through, maintaining hygiene.

Environment: Rabbits are sensitive to heat stress — temperatures above 30°C cause reduced feed intake, infertility, and death. Provide shade (covered structure or under trees), ventilation, and cooling (misting, frozen water bottles) in hot weather. Ideal temperature range: 15–25°C. Protect from rain, wind, and direct sun. Orient housing to catch prevailing breezes.

Nest boxes: Provide each doe with a removable nest box (30 × 40 × 25 cm) 3–4 days before kindling (birth). Line with clean hay or wood shavings. Does pull fur to line the nest. Remove and clean nest box when kits leave the nest (18–21 days).

Feeding

Commercial rabbit pellets: The simplest and most balanced feeding option. Formulated at 16–18% crude protein, 14–18% fibre, 2,400–2,600 kcal DE/kg. Lactating does: 250–400 g/day. Growers: 100–200 g/day. Bucks and dry does: 120–150 g/day.

Roughage: Provide unlimited grass hay (eragrostis or teff) or lucerne hay. Hay supplies essential fibre for gut motility and prevents enteritis. Rabbits are hindgut fermenters — fibre is critical for digestive health.

Fresh greens: Supplement with vegetable scraps, carrot tops, cabbage leaves, spinach, and weeds (avoid poisonous plants — lantana, oleander, avocado leaves). Introduce new greens gradually to prevent diarrhoea.

Water: Always provide fresh, clean water via nipple drinkers or bowls. Lactating does consume 1–2 litres/day. Dehydration rapidly reduces milk production and can kill kits.

Reproduction & Marketing

Reproduction: Does reach sexual maturity at 4–5 months (medium breeds) or 6–8 months (giant breeds). Breed weight, not age: NZW does at 3.5 kg; Californians at 3.0 kg. Gestation: 28–32 days (average 31). Litter size: 6–12 kits (average 8 for NZW). Does can be re-bred 14–35 days after kindling depending on intensity of programme. Intensive: 6–8 litters/year; semi-intensive: 4–6 litters/year. Always take the doe to the buck's cage for mating (does are territorial). Watch for successful mating (buck falls off sideways with a grunt).

Slaughter & marketing: Slaughter at 10–12 weeks (2.0–2.5 kg live weight; 1.1–1.4 kg carcass). Dressing percentage: 52–58%. Market to: restaurants (high-end and traditional), butcheries, farmers' markets, direct to consumers, and export (limited but growing). Rabbit meat marketing requires consumer education — many South Africans are unfamiliar with rabbit as food. Price point: R80–R150/kg retail (higher than chicken, competitive with lamb).

By-products: Rabbit manure is an excellent organic fertiliser — high in nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. Can be applied directly to gardens without composting (unlike chicken manure, it won't burn plants). Rabbit skins can be tanned for crafts, fashion accessories, and lining. Rex pelts command premium prices in the global fur market.

Aquaculture in South Africa

Aquaculture is the fastest-growing food production sector globally, and South Africa has significant untapped potential in freshwater fish farming. The country's warm climate, abundant water resources (in some regions), and growing demand for affordable protein create opportunities for both small-scale and commercial aquaculture. The Department of Agriculture, Land Reform and Rural Development (DALRRD) and the Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment (DFFE) jointly oversee aquaculture development. The main freshwater species farmed are tilapia, African catfish, and trout.

Species

Mozambique tilapia (Oreochromis mossambicus): Indigenous to SA — the only tilapia species that can be legally farmed in most SA provinces without special permits. Moderate growth rate. Hardy and tolerant of poor water quality, low oxygen, and brackish water. Reaches 300–500 g in 6–8 months. Maximum size approximately 600–800 g. The recommended starting species for beginners.

Nile tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus): The world's most farmed tilapia species. Faster growth than Mozambique tilapia — reaches 500–800 g in 6–8 months. Better feed conversion (FCR 1.4–1.8:1). However, Nile tilapia is a Category 2 alien invasive species in SA under NEMBA — farming requires an Alien and Invasive Species (AIS) permit and strict biosecurity measures to prevent escape into natural waterways. Currently permitted in specific areas of Limpopo, Mpumalanga, and KZN (within its natural historical range). Check DFFE regulations before investing.

Production Systems

Pond culture: The simplest and most affordable system. Earthen ponds of 200–1,000 m², 1.0–1.5 m deep. Stock at 3–5 fish/m² for semi-intensive (with feeding) or 1–2 fish/m² for extensive (fertilised ponds, natural food). Ponds require clay soils or liners to hold water. Manage water quality: dissolved oxygen >3 mg/L, pH 6.5–8.5, temperature 25–30°C (optimal). Yields: 5–15 tonnes/ha/year semi-intensive; 1–3 tonnes/ha/year extensive.

Cage culture: Floating net cages in existing dams or reservoirs. Cage sizes typically 4 × 4 × 2 m or 6 × 6 × 2 m. Stock at 20–50 fish/m³. Requires good water flow for oxygen supply and waste removal. Yields: 15–25 kg/m³ per cycle. Cost-effective use of existing water infrastructure. Requires DFFE and DWS authorisation.

Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS): Tank-based systems with water filtration, biofiltration, and recirculation. Highest control over water quality. Water reuse of 90–99%. Allows high stocking densities (30–80 kg/m³). Capital-intensive but year-round production regardless of climate. Growing interest for peri-urban operations near markets.

Feeding & Water Quality

Feeding: Commercial floating pellets (28–35% crude protein depending on fish size). Feed 2–3 times daily at 2–5% of body weight. Reduce feeding in cold weather (below 20°C tilapia feed intake drops sharply; below 15°C they stop feeding). FCR target: 1.5–1.8:1 in well-managed systems. Feed cost represents 50–60% of production cost.

Water quality parameters: Dissolved oxygen >3 mg/L (critical — tilapia die below 1 mg/L). Temperature 25–30°C optimal (growth ceases below 15°C; lethal below 10°C). pH 6.5–8.5. Ammonia (NH₃) below 0.05 mg/L. Nitrite below 0.5 mg/L. Monitor daily with test kits. Aeration (paddlewheels, air blowers) essential in intensive systems.

African Sharptooth Catfish (Clarias gariepinus)

Status: Indigenous to SA and widely distributed. No permit required for farming in most provinces. The most important aquaculture catfish species in Africa.

Physical characteristics: Scaleless, elongated body. Dark grey/brown above, pale below. Accessory air-breathing organ allows survival in low-oxygen and even temporary dry conditions. Can reach 15+ kg but market size is typically 1.0–1.5 kg.

Advantages: Extremely hardy — tolerates poor water quality, low oxygen, high stocking densities, and temperature fluctuations (15–35°C). Fast growth — reaches 1.0 kg in 6–8 months with good feeding. Omnivorous — accepts a wide range of feeds. Air-breathing ability provides a safety margin in low-oxygen events. High fillet yield (38–45%). Can be smoked, dried, or sold fresh.

Limitations: Aggressive and cannibalistic — size grading is essential to prevent larger fish eating smaller ones. Requires regular sorting every 4–6 weeks. Flavour can be "muddy" if grown in poor-quality water (off-flavour from geosmin). Purge in clean water for 3–5 days before harvest. Market acceptance varies — popular in some SA communities, unfamiliar in others.

Production Systems

Ponds: Earthen ponds at 5–20 fish/m². Catfish tolerate high densities due to air-breathing ability. Ponds can be smaller than tilapia ponds (100–500 m²). Yields: 10–30 tonnes/ha/year with intensive feeding.

Tank culture: Concrete, fibreglass, or plastic tanks (1,000–10,000 litres). Stock at 100–300 fish/m³ in flow-through or RAS systems. Easier to manage grading and harvesting than ponds. Popular for small-scale and peri-urban operations.

Feeding: Commercial sinking pellets (35–42% crude protein). Feed 2–3 times daily. FCR 1.2–1.6:1 (better than tilapia). Catfish can also utilise low-cost feeds (chicken offal, abattoir waste, food processing by-products) — however, quality commercial feed produces the best growth and flesh quality.

Rainbow Trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss)

Status: Alien species introduced to SA in the late 1800s for sport fishing. Established in many cooler SA rivers and dams. Classified as a Category 2 alien invasive species under NEMBA — requires an AIS permit for farming. Commercial farming permitted in demarcated areas with biosecurity measures.

Requirements: Trout are coldwater fish — require water temperature of 10–18°C (optimal 14–16°C). Above 20°C, trout become stressed and stop feeding; above 24°C is typically lethal. Dissolved oxygen must be above 6 mg/L (they need more oxygen than warmwater species). Clean, well-oxygenated flowing water is essential.

Where in SA: Limited to cooler regions — Mpumalanga Highveld and escarpment (Dullstroom, Lydenburg, Sabie), KZN Midlands, Eastern Cape highlands, Western Cape mountain areas, and parts of Limpopo (Magoebaskloof, Haenertsburg). Farm sites must have reliable cool, clean water from mountain streams, springs, or boreholes.

Production: Raceways (long, narrow channels with flowing water) are the standard production system. Stock at 15–30 kg/m³ depending on water flow and oxygen. Growth: fingerlings (5–10 g) to market size (300–500 g, portion size) in 10–14 months. FCR 1.2–1.5:1 on high-quality commercial trout pellets (42–48% protein, high-energy sinking pellets). Trout is a high-value product — farm-gate price R60–R100/kg. Markets: restaurants, fresh fish retailers, smoked trout, and recreational fishing dams.

Challenges: Limited suitable locations in SA. Water temperature rising due to climate change threatens some existing operations. High feed cost (trout pellets are the most expensive aquaculture feed). Disease management: bacterial gill disease, furunculosis (Aeromonas), and whirling disease (Myxobolus) require clean water and good biosecurity. Regulatory compliance for alien species farming.

Environmental Guides

Environmental Management & Conservation

Sustainable farming practices and environmental stewardship for Mpumalanga's diverse ecosystems.

Soil Conservation Guide

Mpumalanga's soils face serious erosion threats from intense summer rainfall, especially on degraded or overgrazed land. Protecting soil health is fundamental to long-term agricultural productivity.

Contour banks: Construct earthen contour banks along the slope to slow water runoff. Spacing depends on slope gradient — consult your local extension officer for design.

Grass waterways: Establish grassed channels in natural drainage lines to prevent gully formation. Use Vetiver grass or Kikuyu for its dense root system.

Stone packing/gabions: In areas with existing dongas (gullies), pack stone check dams or install gabion baskets to slow water flow and trap sediment.

Cover crops: Never leave soil bare. Plant cover crops (oats, radish, rye) after harvest to protect against rain splash erosion.

Soil testing: Test soil every 2–3 years through accredited laboratories. Key parameters: pH, phosphorus, potassium, calcium, magnesium, organic carbon, and texture.

Organic matter: Build soil organic matter through composting, mulching, crop residue retention, and green manuring. Target at least 2% organic carbon for productive soils.

Liming: Most Mpumalanga soils are acidic (pH 4.5–5.5). Apply agricultural lime to raise pH to 5.5–6.5 for most crops. Dolomitic lime also supplies magnesium.

Biological health: Encourage soil life — earthworms, mycorrhizal fungi, and beneficial bacteria. Reduce tillage, avoid excessive chemical use, and maintain organic inputs.

Water Resource Management

Water is Mpumalanga's most critical agricultural resource. Efficient use, conservation, and protection of water sources ensures sustainable farming for future generations.

Drip irrigation: Most water-efficient method (90–95% efficiency). Ideal for vegetables, fruit trees, and row crops. Higher upfront cost but significant long-term water savings.

Scheduling: Use soil moisture probes, tensiometers, or weather-based ET calculations to irrigate only when needed. Avoid waterlogging — it wastes water and damages roots.

Maintenance: Clean filters regularly, check for leaks, and flush drip lines. A poorly maintained system can waste more water than it saves.

Rainwater harvesting: Install guttering on farm buildings to collect roof runoff. A 100m² roof in Nelspruit (750mm rainfall) can collect 75,000 litres per year.

Farm dams: Construct small farm dams in suitable locations (with proper authorisation under the National Water Act). Line with clay or geomembrane to reduce seepage.

Wetland protection: Wetlands are protected ecosystems under NEMA. Do not drain, cultivate, or dam wetlands. They provide natural water filtration and flood control.

Biodiversity & Conservation

Mpumalanga is one of South Africa's most biodiverse provinces, home to the Kruger National Park, Blyde River Canyon, and unique grassland ecosystems. Farmers play a vital role in conservation.

Grasslands: Mpumalanga's Highveld grasslands are a threatened ecosystem. Less than 2% is formally protected. Avoid converting natural grassland to cropland — once lost, it takes centuries to recover.

Riparian zones: Maintain natural vegetation within 32m of rivers and streams (legal requirement). Riparian buffers filter runoff, prevent erosion, and provide wildlife habitat.

Indigenous trees: Plant indigenous trees (marula, wild fig, yellowwood) for shade, windbreaks, and ecosystem services. Avoid exotic plantations near water sources.

Pollinators: Protect bee populations — they are essential for fruit, vegetable, and oilseed pollination. Avoid spraying insecticides during flowering. Maintain bee-friendly habitats.

Predator management: Use non-lethal methods where possible — guard dogs, kraal at night, improved fencing. Indiscriminate predator killing disrupts ecosystems.

Bird conservation: Vultures, secretary birds, and blue cranes are threatened. Avoid poison use (especially for predator control — it kills scavengers), and report sightings to BirdLife South Africa.

Climate Adaptation for Agriculture

Climate change is increasing the frequency of droughts, heat waves, and extreme rainfall in Mpumalanga. Adaptive farming strategies help build resilience against these challenges.

Diversify: Don't rely on a single crop or livestock type. Mixed farming systems spread risk across different enterprises.

Drought-tolerant varieties: Choose crop cultivars bred for water stress tolerance. For livestock, indigenous breeds handle drought better than exotic breeds.

Water storage: Build water reserves during good rainfall years. Invest in efficient irrigation and rainwater harvesting infrastructure.

Feed reserves: Stockpile hay and silage during productive seasons. A 3-month feed reserve can carry livestock through drought without panic selling.

No-till: Reduce tillage to maintain soil structure, improve water infiltration, and reduce erosion. Use herbicides for weed control instead of mechanical cultivation.

Crop rotation: Rotate crops to break pest and disease cycles, improve soil health, and optimise nutrient use. Maize-soybean rotation is highly effective.

Mulching: Retain crop residues on the soil surface. Mulch reduces evaporation by 30–50%, moderates soil temperature, and feeds soil organisms.

Agricultural Disaster Risk Management

The DARDLEA sub-programme provides disaster risk reduction support services — including prevention, mitigation, preparedness, response and relief — to producers across Mpumalanga. Early warning advisories are issued monthly via bulk SMS, email, and at Agricultural District Centres. Daily extreme weather warnings from SAWS are accompanied by risk reduction strategies.

D0 — Normal: Short-term dryness typical at drought onset. Slow crop/pasture growth, elevated fire risk. Dam levels 80–100%, groundwater 60–100%. Classified as 1-in-3-year dryness.

D1 — Moderate Drought: Some damage to crops, pastures, livestock. High fire risk. Stream and reservoir levels low; water restrictions may be imposed. Dam levels 60–80%, groundwater 40–60%. Classified as 1-in-5-year drought.

D2 — Severe Drought: Crop, pasture, and livestock losses common. Extremely high fire risk. Water restrictions typically mandatory. Dam levels 40–60%, groundwater 15–30%. Classified as 1-in-10-year drought.

D3 — Extreme Drought: Widespread losses. Water restrictions compulsory. Disaster drought declared with active government response. Dam levels 20–40%, groundwater 15–30%. Classified as 1-in-20-year drought.

D4 — Emergency Drought: Exceptional, widespread crop, pasture and livestock losses. Water shortage emergency across reservoirs, streams, and wells. Dam levels 0–20% (including earth dams), groundwater 0–15%. Classified as 1-in-50-year drought.

Water infrastructure: Drill and equip boreholes. Repair all irrigation equipment. Implement water harvesting techniques and consider grey water reuse.

Crop management: Select drought-tolerant cultivars. Scout and control pests associated with drought conditions (e.g., bollworm, aphids increase under stress).

Livestock management: Keep livestock in balance with available grazing. Provide additional feed and appropriate mineral licks. Implement best veld management practices including rotational grazing and camp systems.

Health: Adhere to relevant vaccinations in line with the season — drought-stressed animals are more susceptible to disease.

Planting strategy: Wait for sufficient moisture before planting winter crops and stay within the normal planting window. Be conservative in planting — reduce density, area, or choose shorter-season cultivars when below-normal rainfall is forecast.

Irrigation: Reduce planting area in line with water restrictions. Repair and optimise irrigation systems before the season.

Livestock: Keep livestock in balance with available grazing. Provide additional feed (e.g., relevant mineral licks). Ensure enough water points and shelter during cold spells.

Veld fire risk: Veld recovers in summer but dries out in winter, dramatically increasing fire risk. Prioritise creation and maintenance of fire belts. Adhere to veld fire warnings from SAWS and local fire protection associations.

Stay informed: Follow weather and climate forecasts regularly to make informed decisions. Implement strategies from monthly advisories and daily SAWS warnings.

Protect infrastructure: Lift water pumps from the flood line to higher ground. Remove all irrigation equipment from flood plains. Close irrigation systems during flooding events.

Field management: Do not apply production inputs during active flooding. Avoid planting in the flood line or close to river banks. Create waterways on access roads and contours to prevent damming and silting of earth dams.

Dam safety: Open spillways to reduce pressure on earth dam walls (flood-uncontrolled dams). Unblock all storm water drainage systems.

Crop protection: Use hail net to cover horticultural crops to reduce hailstorm damage.

Livestock safety: Move livestock from grazing camps to safe, high-lying areas. Provide shelter where possible. Vaccinate against Rift Valley fever and botulism (diseases associated with flooding conditions).

Stay informed: Keep up to date with advice from credible institutions — DARDLEA, SAWS, and local agricultural organisations.

Monthly advisories: The National Agro-meteorological Committee issues early warning information and disaster risk reduction measures monthly. These advisories highlight precautionary measures for upcoming conditions.

Dissemination: Advisories are sent via bulk SMS, email, and fax to registered farmers. They are also available at Agricultural District Centres and on the DALRRD website.

Daily weather warnings: DARDLEA relays daily extreme weather warnings from the South African Weather Service (SAWS), accompanied by specific risk reduction strategies for farmers.

How to register: Contact your district agricultural office or disaster coordinator to be added to the early warning distribution list. Ehlanzeni: Mgiba Hillarian, Gert Sibande: Luhlanga Forgiveness, Nkangala: Mbiza Bongeikile, Bohlabela: Khumbuza Vutomi.

Invasive Species Management

Invasive alien plants consume vast quantities of water and displace indigenous vegetation. Land owners are legally obligated to control listed invasive species under NEMBA (National Environmental Management: Biodiversity Act).

Lantana (Lantana camara): Aggressive invader in bushveld and forest margins. Toxic to livestock. Control by cutting and treating stumps with registered herbicide.

Bugweed (Solanum mauritianum): Prevalent in mist-belt forests and plantation edges. Spread by birds. Remove seedlings by hand; cut and treat mature trees.

Wattle species (Acacia mearnsii, A. dealbata): Major water-consuming invaders. Dense stands reduce streamflow by up to 80%. Ring-bark large trees or fell and treat stumps.

Triffid weed (Chromolaena odorata): Spreading rapidly through the Lowveld. Smothers indigenous vegetation. Requires sustained control through cutting, burning, and herbicide.

Working for Water: The government's Working for Water programme provides free labour for invasive plant clearing on qualifying properties. Contact your local Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment (DFFE) office.

Biological control: Several biocontrol agents have been released for species like lantana, bugweed, and water hyacinth. These reduce the vigour of invasive plants without chemicals.

Integrated approach: Combine mechanical removal, chemical treatment, and fire management. Follow-up is critical — invasive plants return aggressively from seed banks if not monitored.

Legal obligation: Under NEMBA, failure to control listed invasive species on your property can result in fines. Keep records of control activities.
